I've never had a problem with my Radeon 9800XT. With that said, I will say that at this point in the graphics card wars, I would buy an nvidia of the current generation. I hear the 6700 is supposed to be a great balance of price and performance, but I don't know if it's available yet.

At this point, ATI and Nvidia both have excellent support in Linux (with the exception of ATI laptop display adapters due to the heavy modification laptop manufacturers do to them). Go with what runs what you want to play the best and matches your budget. I look at the price/performance factor and go for the best bang for my buck. You pay for performance, so you have to figure out what is acceptable for you.

As for drives, they are based on an industry standard. With that said, drives tend to also perform up to their price tag. With that said, I got the cheapest dvd-rom and cd-rw I could find on newegg and I've been very pleased with them. The one thing I think spending a little more would give you is a quieter drive and possibly a longer lasting drive. The cheap drives are cheap for a reason.

FX5700LE
GPU Clock: 250MHz
RAM Clock: 400MHz
Max amount of video ram: 256mb

FX5700
GPU Clock: 425MHz
RAM Clock: 550MHz
Max amount of video ram: 256mb

now with that much of difference in both core and ram clock speeds, a FX5700 128mb model would be able to keep up with and with most games it would outrun the FX5700LE 256mb model, but a good solid FX5700 256mb model will run you about $164

also as a side note, the FX5700LE cards run the same core speed (250MHz) as a FX5200 card except for ram amount/speed, but the avg fx5200 card runs 400MHz of 128mb ddr ram

Post by: crowe on October 05, 2004, 03:23:33 pm
Not to discourage you, but why go with 64-bit for a gaming rig?

Your games won't gain all that much of an advantage -- they'd run in 32-bit mode.  The only perfomance gain would be in other areas: An increased bus speed, faster memory, etc.

Like I said, not trying to discourage you, I'm just trying to understand why you're going 64-bit when you could easily put together a beefier 32-bit gaming machine for the same amount of money.


That having been said, I will agree with the part about not skimping on a power supply.  Cheap power supplies tend to fail in rather catastrophic ways;  that is, they not only crash and burn, but also take anything attached to them in the process.

Having just built a system earlier this year, I would suggest not skimping on video card. I spent a good deal on a Radeon 9800XT and plan on buying a new motherboard and pci-x video card, with plans on new processor the following spring.

My theory on building a gaming system, start with your video card and buy as much as you can afford now. Geforce 6800 GDDR 128MB (not GT or Ultra) are running about $270. Save up a little more since it seems you are buying piece by piece and it will go a long way.

If you're planning on making this a gaming system, you need to make sure you have a quality hard drive. There is a good bit of research I did in choosing my drive as I can tell a huge difference between a cheaper 80GB in one of my systems and the 200GB Seagate I have in my A64 box. It definitely makes a difference in games like Doom3.

Also, I bought a cheap power supply and had terrible power issues. Voltage was very unstable, so I spent about $100 on an Antec Truepower and haven't looked back.

When you say "gaming" system, you are opening yourself up to a higher level of spending. If you expect to run the newer games, video card and system memory are probably the two most important factors, followed by hard drive and processor. Last year, I was playing ET on a P3 550 with 512 MB SDRAM, 17 GB 5400 RPM IBM drive and GeforceFX 5200.
