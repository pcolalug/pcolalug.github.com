Certifications losing impact
« on: May 01, 2006, 05:02:55 pm »
In case you missed it on /., an eweek article is reporting that for the first time employers are paying non-certified employees more than certified employees. This goes to show that employers are becoming more aware that just because you have a piece of paper, doesn't mean you know the job, nor you are flexible to new technologies. I've long been a believer that a broad understanding of the core concepts of your given area of expertise is much more valuable that knowing a specific application. I have taken certification tests and I have studied for ones that I never took and it there are rarely any questions that are more than regurgitation of facts.

If you really want to get a job in IT, don't waste your money on certifications. Learn the basics and go from there. If an employer really wants you to have a cert, they'll pay for the test, if not the training or study material.

I understand what you are saying, but I have found that certified techs tend to know less than techs without certs, given that they have the same years of experience.  I tend to prefer non-certified to certified typically, though you always judge by the individual, not by the pieces of paper.

Certs/degrees are what help you get through HR. A good IT manager shouldn't care as much about what pieces of paper a person has and be able to tell whether or not the applicant is capable of the job. Are they smart? What kind of background do they have? Can they learn new things quickly? Do they have a good base of knowledge?

Where most IT certifications go wrong is anyone can go take the test. There are some that require a level of experience prior to taking the test, and those are the ones with the most value. The market has changed since 2000 though and people who are doing hiring aren't as desperate as they were before. They know the difference between someone that knows what they're doing and some Joe that did sales and then got his MCSE because there was good money in IT.

There are times when companies have a requirement for folks with certain certifications. The DoD and contractors of the DoD are now coming under a new policy that certain positions REQUIRE some sort of professional certification, uniformed personnel included. That's why I was sent to the SANS class.

alan, if you're having a hard time finding a job due to lack of experience or knowledge, try looking into private consulting. Since I don't get to work with unix/linux enough in my regular job, I am bolstering the experience I can list on my resume with consulting work. The bonus is that I get paid well doing it and don't have to put much time into it. I work as much as I like and it ends up being pretty much a win/win for me.

Also, you aren't going to hurt any feelings by applying for jobs that you don't really fit all of the requirements on. A lot of times, when you submit a resume and you don't necessarily fit the job you are applying for, companies will look to see if there is something you may have a better fit with. If they see potential in your learning ability, then they may pick you up anyway. Provided you have a good base of knowledge, you should be able to adapt and pick up new knowledge quickly. There is always a period of time that a company expects you to adjust and learn how they do things and what they are working with.

this is how i have survived in the tech game since getting my ba in 1993 from uwf.

1. go where the jobs are plenty, so that if you don't have alot of experience, but hr is looking for bodies for entry level, you have a better shot. took me 2 weeks of "pounding the pavement" in atlanta. my manager who hired me is still a good friend today, 14 years later. he is a director of it down in o-town now. i'm currently a network administrator working for a top five defense contractor on eglin. who knew??? when i started looking for a job back in 1993 in atlanta, I had NO local contacts, save my uncle and friends letting me crash on the couch as I hunted for employment.  I haven't had to do that since the internet took over, and hr finds me now.

2. once you are in the door. learn everything you can. i mean volunteer for the server room cleanup. sit in those dead head vendor meetings that last all day (hopefully catered). take those accounts, or customers, or jobs that no one wants to do, and SHINE in them.

3. i'm all for small biz, but you need to get some "named" companies, w. "big" projects on your resume.. i'm not talking "helped install outlook and repaired customers .pst's and saved their shitty small business from failing". i'm talking, lead project engineer on deploying enterprise wide computer telephony integration solution and lowering tco for telecommunications infrastructre utilizing VoIP.. or even "saved company x amount of dollars" by "integrating network faxing solutions which reduced time/energy/effort/logistics/equipment/paper/maintenance costs to the company" or "onsite support dba/lan/wan administrator for a Windows 2000/ASP/IIS based intranet registration solution supporting up to 200,000 processed customers within three-five days" (that's one of mine btw).

4. as for the certs. i've run into plenty of "paper tigers" in my days. been burned by a couple of them, but was able to recover (thank God we backed up the ms sql database every night, but thats another story).

5. college degree gets your foot in the door. what you do w. the job once you are in (the things BEYOND doing above and beyond in the job, such as making industry contacts, expanding your infinite network via yahoo! gmail, myspace, LinkedIn (great resource) participating in local trade or industry groups or WHATEVER) you need to CONTINUE to network w. others in your field, and find out where the jobs will be BEFORE they becone available, so that when it comes time to hop the lily pad (which that time always comes, whether its 4 years or 14 years) you'll be ready to leap.

That's about it! The technical stuff, if you are intelligent, thoughtful, aggressive, and a "go to" guy, you'll do well. Big sacrifices up front, going the distance and more will reap BIG rewards. Trust me, I'm living it.
