1400  Distributions / RPM Based Distributions / RedHat 8.0 - First Impressions on: October 08, 2002, 02:26:58 pm
It just seems like a wasted effort for the Gnome and KDE projects to continue in parallel.  To me it looks like they're both working hard to incorporate the same features into their own product.

Things are a lot better now than they used to be.  I remember up until a few short years ago that something as trivial as copy->pasting between applications in X was a nitemare.  

I guess part of my frustration is that XWindows is sort of old technology, and was missing a lot of core functionality that you needed on the desktop (like sound servers, a shared clipboard for copy->pasting, a good general file manager), and each of the larger Window Managers (Gnome/KDE) implemented them in their own way.  I agree that we probably will not see the merging of the two, since each (in my view anyway) have probably equal share on the linux desktops.  

Ultimately I'd like it see the window manager code abstraced from the system code.  Someone mentioned it above.  That way if you want to code in the KDE/QT style that's fine, and if you code in Gnome/GTK that's fine, but under the hood the library calls would be converted to some more generic library that handles actually calling the x server and performing functions.

I don't know if anyone else has noticed, but if you are in gnome and want to start a KDE application, you've got a substantial wait ahead of you.  While the DCOP server, ArtS server, and other desktop components fire up before the application can start.  I assume it is similar trying to start a large Gnome app from within KDE (though I haven't tried, so I'm not positive on that).

I thought I might step on some toes with this subject, but at least we're generating some interesting discussions

1401  Misc / Other Misc / Mediacom is sucking right about now... on: October 08, 2002, 02:11:37 pm
I live in Gulf Breeze, and had Mediacom cable ISP for about two years.  I had tons of problems with it, especially service going off for hours at a time (and being on hold for over an hour with tech support was not uncommon).  

Most of the time they'd come out and couldn't figure out what was wrong and resort to "Looks like the modem is bad, I'll have to bring a replacement back out tomorrow".  I went through 5 modems including the old surfboards and the newer RCA.

Anyway, I finally got fed up and switched to BellSouth FastAccess DSL about 6 months ago.  No problems, no downtime.  (Actually I was down for about 2 hours a few months ago, but no problem - they provide you with a backup dial-up account for use in those situations.  My router is setup so that as soon as my DSL goes offline my modem dials up the backup, I don't notice a loss of service -- except for the slowdown).  

No blocked ports so I can host websites if I like (all the pictures I link to are sitting on my router).  

And it turns out to be about the same price.  One surprise was they gave me a USB modem.  My router is an old pentium 166 NEC machine, so I had to go buy a $15 USB card for it, worked perfectly in Linux though.

Definitely check them out.  With my UPS backups and DSL I didn't even loose internet service through the hurricane(s).  It was pretty nice.

That seems to be the idea with RedHat's latest effort.  I did a fairly minimal install.  Barely installed software off 2 of the 5 cd's you get.  So there are literally thousands of packages you can go through and add as you like.

I read through the Lindows articles posted on another forum.  Looks very nice, but I have to agree with the author.  It's time for some of the linux folks to quit writing so many competing applications and focus on creating the best User Interface available.  Apple took a big chance with OS/X but it paid off (I really like the look, but I could see getting tired off it after looking at it every day).  

Some cohersion needs to happen in the Open Source world.  KDE and GNOME are each killer desktops in their own right, but just imagine what would be available if all the coders put that effort into one single desktop.  Same for other apps, there are like 20 different Instant Messangers available.  GAIM has by far pulled ahead, has tons of features, and works great.   The other projects are just to amuse the few people who use them I guess.  

A lot of people are completely against the idea of combining the efforts of gnome / kde into one project.  Well in that case I would say let them keep developing independent desktops, but have the same compatibilities at the core.  Same configuration files, same menu layouts, (SAME SOUND SERVER!!!).  That way even if you're running KDE, your GNOME apps will still function perfectly.

And on the issue of sound servers, some of my programs expect to see ArtsD, some expect to see ESD, and others want direct access to the sound card (requiring me to kill off the sound servers all together).  Why doesn't linux push the sound server to a lower level than the window manager, so your window manager's programs, and any console programs all go through a system level sound server?  Seems the logical (and best) solution to me.

Maybe someone can offer some input into this.  As far as I can tell (from what I've used) open office is the best office suite available for linux.  I tried KOffice but didn't really feel comfortable using it, AbiWord is kinda limited in it's capabilities.  That's another area where developers of other less capable products could abandon their current efforts and concentrate on making the best programs even better.

Back on the Lindows issue.  I like what I saw in the review, but I'm not about to pay for a linux distro.  Sorry, starving student budget here.  Why don't they have a free version available like most everyone else has?  Give away the OS but charge your subscription (maybe $25 a year, not $125 or $99 or whatever) for the software installer (click & run or whatever it was called).

--

Another issue.  Microsoft clearly has some good (albeit bloated) programs.  Internet Explorer does a great job, as does Microsoft Office.  The programs have been ported to Mac and Mac OSX.  Even Internet Explorer and Media Player have been ported to Unix (Sparc I beleive).  Would be even easier to release x86 linux ports.  From what I undesrstand, Microsoft's flagship money-maker is the office suite.  Why not port it to linux and gain that platform as well?  It can't be because they don't want people to consider switching from windows, they offer it on Mac.  Oh well, just a thought.

1408  Distributions / Other Linux Distributions / Anyone else use Slack? on: October 07, 2002, 03:51:50 pm
I have used many different versions of slackware.  Right now slackware is powering my router / web server.  

Seems to be a nice little distro.  Weak package management though, I think they should adopt RPM which is sort of a de-facto standard now anyway.  That would allow Ximian to release their desktop for it.

Releases are very slow compared to other distros, expecially in the last two years.

If you're used to the nice graphical installers of mdk / rh / suse you probably won't like slackware.  The install is text based (ncurses) and not very user friendly.  A little cumbersome.  About like installing FreeBSD if you've ever tackled that.  Definitely not for a beginner since you should know how to partition your drive with linux's fdisk.

On the plus side it has a lot of nice tools for the console user (why I'm using it for my router).  Nice utilities like NetWatch which other distros don't care to include.  And a text editor called "pico" that a friend likes to use (if you ask me, you never need more than vi for quick editing jobs, and emacs for web browsing / authoring )    

They also seem to be sort of a purist distro.  They don't seem to molest things like KDE and GNOME as much as the others do  That could be a good thing or a bad thing depending on what you like.

If you know what you're doing, don't mind compiling your own apps from source, (and know how to customize the kernel, non-modular last time I checked) ,then you might just like it.
1409  Distributions / RPM Based Distributions / RedHat 8.0 - First Impressions on: October 07, 2002, 03:41:09 pm
Well RedHat has obviously customized it a bit, but generally the menu has always been fairly well organized.  But it was definitely cleaned up and fine-tuned for this release.

I was trying to show off one aspect in the screenshot.  They've trimmed out all the extraneous programs.  The internet menu used to consist of Mozilla, Netscape 6, Netscape 4, Kate, Licq, Gaim, kirc, and a bunch of other programs.  Now they've gone back to basics, they give you one web browser (mozilla), one chat program (gaim), and one email program (evolution).

This is all in gnome's push to create a more user-friendly desktop.  That's one thing that makes windows easy for beginners, you want to go online you click on internet explorer, want to edit a document, click on Word.  OpenOffice has been integrated now as gnome's official office suite, evolution (which is an excellent personal management / email tool) is part of the official packages, and with some big-name backers adopting it like Sun Microsystems, Gnome is well on it's way.  

Kind of the opposite of my experiences with KDE lately.  I don't know if they're pushing ahead with version releases too fast or what, but I've had serious stability problems with KDE3 and konqueror in particular.  In fact, yesterday my entire mandrake system took a nose  dive (prompting me to download and install RedHat).  Here's a screenshot of what I found last time I booted my mandrake machine.  My programs were gone from the menu system, and all my quick launch icons werer now corrupt.
