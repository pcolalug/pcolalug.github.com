I agree with you about we live in a mixed economy, otherwise farm, dairy, oil & gas industries would have to charge what it really cost to produce their products (And you thought $3.59 a gallon was high for milk). Oddly, I have no problem with that, as it does have a benefit to all consumers. What I don't agree with you is your statement "The businesses with the best strategies would form monopolies and make it nearly impossible for any potential competitor to enter the market.". Short of buying all of your competition, you can't "form" a monopoly. Does Microsoft have a monopoly? Not at all. Last time I checked RealNetworks and Apple are still their own entities. Is Microsoft guilty of anti-competitive behaviour? Absolutely, as they essentially forced vendors to install their software (and not install their competitions) or be cut out completely. Did Microsoft get put in their place? Yes, as vendors are now willing to provide systems with other OS (such as Dell's w/RHEL) without fear of retribution from Microsoft. Do I consider "bundling" (including a browser and media player with the OS) a bad thing? Absolutely not, provided it does not prevent me from installing a 3rd party application which last time I checked, Quicktime and Firefox ran just fine even though WMP & IE are installed by default. If it was a bad idea, then why does most linux distributions include Totem & Firefox with the default install? Convenience, and that is what Microsoft is guilty of - making applications convenient.

If what you said was true, then Linux wouldn't have a chance. So far, that is only true in the server realm, as there are way more Linux installs than Windows NT/2000/2003 servers. As Linux on the desktop becomes more mature, and more mainstream apps are either ported to Gnome/KDE, an equal shift in the market share will take place. Companies like Redhat and Novell are working this angle, as the typical cubicle mole only needs a web browser, an email client and an office suite to be productive. Can you say Firefox, Thunderbird & OpenOffice? The best thing that has happened for Linux on the desktop is Vista. I would bet one of the top 3 reasons Vista has been delayed isn't due to security issues, but the fear that once Vista rolls out and support for XP begins to taper off, companies will then have a REASON to migrate to something other than Microsoft. It almost happened in the transition from 2000 to XP, but Desktop Linux wasn't mature enough yet. Now that it is, Microsoft has plenty of reason to keep milking the XP cash cow as long as possible.

Now take Apple for example - Again another competitor that has been able to make it without having to whine about Microsoft being a monopoly.
Granted, I believe they exist strictly for those people who despise Microsoft so much, they are willing to put their money where their mouth is. If more people would do this, Microsoft wouldn't be able to "bastardize" the protocols as you put it and call it "innovation". At the end of the day, my point remains there are too many companies that would rather cry foul about not having a "fair market" to work in instead of putting forth the effort to just make a better product.

Quote from: jaymz_720 on February 17, 2006, 12:46:51 pm
      I don't think pi was calculated on a 32b either.

It can be calculated on anything from 4bit+. Keyword is "calculated", not stored in a variable. For your project, don't rely on the precision of the variables, but examine the method of how you would determine the correct answer. You'll probably find that all you need are simple integers and allocate space to store the result. As you keep iterating through the process refining the answer to higher precision, you'll more than likely either dump the result to screen, or store in a char type location. With this method, your precision is only constrained by the amount of memory you have.

30  Misc / Newbie Corner / Can't seem to get apache working right on: September 26, 2005, 02:28:06 pm
Interesting... I was able to hit those servers from here as well. Try changing...

Code:

<IfDefine !APACHEPROXIED>
Listen *:80
Listen *:400
</IfDefine>


to

Code:

<IfDefine !APACHEPROXIED>
Listen 80
Listen 400
</IfDefine>


Also, you don't want to use virtual hosts unless you are running multiple sites and have proper DNS requests. Set your directive from

Code:

ServerRoot /etc/httpd


to

Code:

ServerRoot /home/web/www/thejernigans/main


Remember that when the No-IP request is not asking for the website by name (www.thejernigans.net), but by IP. Apache uses the actual HTTP GET header in order to determine which site is being requested. In the httpd.conf you posted, the HTTP header will never match because www.thejernigans.net does not resolve to your IP.
