The only reason it is Windows only (could work on Mac I suppose) is because it just opens IE, meaning IE must be installed. So rather than copying and pasting a URL from Firefox to IE, you right-click anywhere on the page and select "View this page in IE", or something along those lines, and the page you are currectly viewing in Firefox magically pops up in IE.

woodyharold, for future reference, if you run into problems with the extension site because of an old version, you can change a few settings to trick Firefox into reporting that it is a newer version than it really is. This would be necessary if you are running a distribution, like Ubuntu, that backports the security updates from the newer version into the older versions of Firefox.

Anyhow, to change your reported version, in your address bar, type about:config and hit enter. This brings up the low level config for Firefox. In the Filter Bar, type general.useragent.vendorSub, or just find it in the list. Once you find it, double-click on the name and a window should pop up, to which you should change the value in the box to the current version of Firefox. Once again, only do this if you know security updates are being backported for your distribution and you are sure you have installed them.

And one final note, Firefox sets up your profile for you under the .mozilla directory in your home directory. Since just before version 1.0, you could upgrade safely without worrying that your profile would get corrupted. I backup selected portions of my Firefox profile in my weekly backup scripts, such as my bookmarks, and they are located in that directory.

Ah...where to begin. Basically, browser devs choice to implement certain standards, leave out others. They also implement certain "features" that they cook up themselves, but are not defined in W3C or other standards. They are proprietary features that are completely optional for webpage designers to use. Sometimes, they are great if you expect all of the visitors to your page to use that browser and no other.

Such is the case with the Pensacola News Journal and FEMA pages. Those pages use a server side technology that checks the User Agent (browser/os type) and if they see a browser they have chosen not to work, know it doesn't work and have no intention of supporting, they tell you to use the browser they designed the page for. This is horrible practice. It isn't that hard to make a web site that is standards compliant and works in at least the three major browsers; IE, Gecko-based (Mozilla, Firefox, Galeon, Konqueror, Safari, shall I go on?) and Opera. What it sounds like is they are too lazy to have three windows open to check their design and use that template for the rest of their dynamically created pages. You design it once, it works with everything and you go on with your life.

By denying your browser, they are saying that your business isn't good enough for them. They expect you to pay "the Microsoft tax" before they can take your money. They know anything other than IE won't work and knowingly turn you away. That is wrong and I think we should do something about it. Perhaps we could draft a form letter to be sent to the PNJ editor. As for the FEMA page, I'm sure letters to our local representatives in the house and senate will get some attention, especially considering the flak that agency has been under lately.

Just so it is clear, the use of User Agent detection can be used to dynamically present a page that will work with that browser. In most cases, the proprietary features have a work-a-like feature on the competing browsers. It's as simple as checking for that browser type and providing the user the page that renders correctly. It's just utter laziness.
