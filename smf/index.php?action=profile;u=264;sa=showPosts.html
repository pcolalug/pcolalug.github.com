1  Misc / The Big Debate / Re: Kororaa Violates GPL? on: May 23, 2006, 10:36:40 pm
You are right about the bigger issue being whether or not we are going to allw non-free code on our systems.  IMHO, it is almost certainly a slippery slope, and we could end up at the bottom of the slope only marginally better off than Windows users.  The binary blobs could contain anything, really.  It doesn't take a lot of Googling to see that it's the OpenBSD folks who are taking the most militant (or at least loudest) stand in this matter.  "Anti-Blob " is sort of the theme of their 3.9 release.

Link to an OpenBSD user blog (?) http://undeadly.org/cgi?action=article&sid=20060519194122  That post drew some interesting commentary (both pro and con) as well as this link to a hypothetical scenario at the bottom of the slope:. http://www.ussg.iu.edu/hypermail/linux/kernel/0512.0/0972.html.

You may have also seen the Kernal Trap interview with Theo deRaadt who had interesting revelations and observations as to which h/w vendors supply documentation adequate to produce open source drivers and why: http://www.ussg.iu.edu/hypermail/linux/kernel/0512.0/0972.html.

It is impossible to over look the big red swirl under your name.  Debian, too, takes a highly principled stand against binary blobs, too, does it not?  There was a bit of a discussion on blobs in Debian last year, with the conclusion that they belonged in non-free, from this thread: http://lists.debian.org/debian-legal/2005/04/msg00047.html.  Actually, I got more Google hits on "Debian + binary blob" than "Open BSD + binary blob."   

Fortunately for me, nothing I do with Linux or the BSDs as a home user requres h/w accelleration.  I have been using Sarge off and on since it was still testing, Ubuntu, for example, is flashier, but not substantively better than Sarge for me--except on this box (amd64), which I hope the release of Etch will address. 

With respect to expectations, I'm not so sure many Widows users have very high expectations anymore.  Most folks I know use their home boxes for e-mail, research & surfing, all of which are just as easy -and much safer-on Linux or FreeBSD.  (Personally, I consider the lack of built-in Flash support a plus when researching/surfing.)  Most of my friends, who are in their 50s (like me) or older seldom bother with games anymore.  Were it possible to persuade more of them to at least try an open source O/S, many of them could easily switch.  My 2¢.

Interesting topic. Bedtime, for me, though.

4  Distributions / Other Operating Systems / Re: FreeBSD Aims for Desktop on: May 17, 2006, 09:48:27 pm
I have installed FreeBSD desktops based on versions  4.8, 4.9  and 4.10.  As a desktop it worked OK, although my recollection of it reminds me of Debian Stable for some reason, older packages, perhaps.  Then I had nothing but problems trying to get the 5.x series to run, as I could never get X11 to work properly (I was missing an important post-install step: no .xinitrc file).  Finally got 6.0 installed with X11 & a KDE desktop, and sound to boot.  It still reminds me of Debian or even Slackware but with a dependency-aware (sometimes) package management.  FreeBSD tends to boot very quickly and it shuts down in a heartbeat,  It also  it seems to be very powerful, although that is just one of those gut impressions, that I couldn't back with an objective measurement if my life depended on it.

Actually, PC-BSD really is FreeBSD with a slicker installer, which leaves one with X11 and sound fully configured & other little niceties.  Package installation is very easy with the .pbi system (as long as they work) and one can still use the traditional FreeBSD pkg_add and ports system.  Be that as it may, for some reason, maybe because it was the first non-graphical install of any *nix I did that actually worked (or masochism?), I'm really fond of the Old Red Mare, & keep diddling with it.   I've downloaded install disks for FreeBSD 6.1 & will probably dance with it some more later this month.

6  Distributions / Debian Based Distrtibutions / Debian Installation on: January 27, 2005, 11:31:33 pm
Looks like I'm way late here, but the easiest way I have found to get a good Debian install is with the net install CD (about 107-113MB).  It can be downloaded from the Debian site.  It installs a base systwem, then depending on what one wants (desktop O/S, server, database, or whatever) downloads and installs the rest.  Broadband is a must

I installled this system 31 Dec. came with KDE and Gnome (2. & soon got an upgrade on KDE.  Uses the Gnome login manager (gdm) to choose between GNOME and KDE,  If gdm is not installed or gets removed it defaults to GNOME at startx command.  I never got a system I could use from the "Woody"  CDs: at best command-line only (& I'm too ignorant for that) or GRUB would hang.

Like others pointed out, the safest way to do Debian upgrades is "smart upgrade" with synaptic, or (at the command line) with apt-get update followed by apt-get distr-upgrade , as those routes seem to avoid dependency and/or incompatibility problems.
