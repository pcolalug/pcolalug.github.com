One question that gets asked a lot is "Why isn't Linux MORE popular?"
It's more stable than competing OSes.
It's mare secure than competing OSes.
It's definitely cheaper than competing OSes.

There's been many reasons given including:

Difficulty of use
Lack of Games
Lack of commercial software support

I have a different reason today. One more reason Linux hasn't swept the country is it's lack of availability in Stores. When you walk into Wal-Mart, Circuit City, or Best Buy you see shelves and shelves of MS software and maybe one or two packages of a Linux distro. Why is this? I think it's because the current majority of Linux users would rather download their OS for free or purchase it online for pennies than to show their support for their favorite OS by buying from a local retailer. Where do most users go when they want that MS OS or Game? A good many of them go to their local retailer. That makes it beneficial for the retailer to carry it in stock. There's a reason that Best Buy has 100s of copies of the latest game on it's shelves. It’s because people are buying it.

Where is all of this going? Well, I'd like to see if we as Linux users can try to make it a point to start buying our Linux OSes locally when possible. This will show a demand for the product and the stores will start ordering more of it. When they order more of it, they put more on the shelf. When there are more copies on the shelf, more consumers notice it. This is how it can gain popularity. When the MS user sees 100 copies of Mandrake, SuSE, or other Linux distro on a shelf, they are bound to notice it and start asking questions.

**Last paragraph contains the short answer**

You have a good point (though, I don't think it will happen).

This weekend I was talking with my brother who has heard me discuss the glories of Linux ad naseum. After he decided he wanted to try it, he asked me where he could buy a copy. His typical thought was to go to a store and buy it. That is where the average computer user thinks to get software. Of course, I told him where to download it.

One of the reasons I am in this game is because of the "cheapness" of the software. I don't mind paying for software if I have to, but if it is not a requirement, then I don't want to do it. My personal opinion on Linux distros is that they should charge and not give it away. But since they are giving it away, I am going to take advantage of it. What I think they should charge though is far less than what they do charge in the stores.

I think they ought to charge $20 to download a distro. This is cheap enough that just about anyone can afford it. Right now they are not making any money off of most of us. $20 would be a windfall for them. I realize that you cannot expect to sell on store shelves with a nice box and book for $20, but you can sell your distro on the web for that. And I would pay it, but not if I don't have to legally.

I am against software piracy (not to say I am better than anyone else and that I have never done it), but Linux/OSS has given me an opportunity to play with my computer and be able to afford it. If I was stuck in a Windows world without OSS, I would probably become just a user and spend my hobby time in other pursuits, like my ham radio where the thrill of building something for cheap is still alive and well. For me, Linux provides a cheap hobby. Therefore, I am not willing to pay $85 for Mandrake's PowerPack, or  $66 / year for their basic club membership.

I bought PowerPack one time because it included a commercial Quicken type software. It was typical of the financial software of the time (three years ago), it was half baked and definately not a Quicken replacement (we now have some good programs, but didn't back then). It was definately not worth the $70 I paid for it. I could buy Qucken for half that, and it was a known good program. I was taking a $70 bet on something that I lost. You can argue that I was buying so much more than one program, but actually I wasn't. Yes, I was getting more, but I paid money because I wanted that one program that I could not get from the download edition.

I agree that it could be a significant boost to Linux if it was available in the retail marketplace more, but I just don't see it happening. The main distros have made a mistake by making their software freely downloadable. It would take some time to turn that corner.

My model: Penguin Distro comes out with a new version in January. Sell it on the store shelves through February (6 or 8 weeks). Then make it a $20 download from then on until the next version is out, while still selling in the store. Anyone that is really hard core and wants to stay ahead of the curve for cheap can get an older version and upgrade all the parts themselves and forget about the nice package (isn't that your typical Slackware guy anyway?).

I am not a Mandrake club member, I am willing to wait until their new version comes out as a public download. And if it doesn't, then I can do without.

The short answer, it is a good thought, but it probably won't happen through me. I am into Linux and computers as a hobby. My PalmOne handheld computer has all the power that I really need to live my life. Of course this is just one opinion. I am curious to see what others have to say.
