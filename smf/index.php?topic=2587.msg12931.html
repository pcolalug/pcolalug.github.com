I know Gentoo has a method to initialize multiple things are once, but I haven't really looked into it for any other distro. The parallel initialization did speed things up considerably, but I have found that the slowest part of my systems boot process is mounting the filesystems. Here is my layout...

Code:
/dev/hda1  ext3  456M  41%  /
/dev/hda2  ext3  228M  9%  /boot
/dev/hda10  reiserfs  99G  5%  /home
/dev/hda7  reiserfs  2.8G 5% /opt
/dev/hda8  reiserfs  2.8G 2% /srv
/dev/hda6  reiserfs  4.7G 1% /tmp
/dev/hda9  reiserfs  14G 15% /usr
/dev/hda5  reiserfs  4.7G 49% /var
/dev/hdb1  ext3  184G 55% /media/storage


It takes about 20 to 30 seconds to check the reiser filesystems and then mount them. It's unreal. Once those get done, gdm is up within ten to fifteen seconds.

Ran across this article on Distrowatch, which talks about some excellent boot time improvements in the upcoming Mandriva release:

Quote
What an exciting week this was! Besides a stable release of GNOME 2.12 and a beta version of Firefox 1.5, four major Linux distributions have entered the final stages of their development cycles - Mandriva Linux 2006, Slackware Linux 10.2, SUSE Linux 10.0 and Ubuntu Linux 5.10. Of these, which one is going to be the winner? While Ubuntu seems like a distribution with the most new features, it is Mandriva's latest beta release that caught our attention during the past week. The reason? The breathtaking boot speed.

That's right - while many distributions have been talking about speeding up the boot process, an area where no Linux distribution compares favourably with Microsoft Windows, it seems that the developers of Mandriva did not just talk - they simply did it! And the result? On our Pentium 4 test box with 384MB of RAM, Mandriva 2006 RC1 takes 23 seconds to boot into the console login prompt, and 52 seconds into full KDE (bypassing the KDM login screen)! This is a remarkable achievement when compared to SUSE Linux 10.0 RC1, which takes 108 seconds to boot into KDE, or Fedora Core, which needs 76 seconds to boot into GNOME on the same system.

Quote from: Brent
I would like to know how they did this, because it certianly wouldn't be easy.


The basic idea is to let multiple services startup concurrently in the background instead of waiting for 1 to start, then starting 2, then starting 3...

For example on my machine, it takes about 10 seconds for the network cards to all pull DHCP information.  By letting those happen in the background, other startup tasks don't have to wait on it, right there would save 10 seconds off my boot time.

Quote from: Brent
23 seconds would be about par for slackware (on my machine).  But it's absolutely blazing for mandrake.  Especially considering how many extra services mandrake starts at boot.


Actually, that is a common misconception made by people who don't use Mandrake.  During the Mandrake install, you choose which services you want to start, so any "extra services" that get started are the result of poor/improper installation.  As a matter of fact, a person has significantly more control over this with Mandrake than they do with a Slackware installation.

Just wanted to clear up that point...
