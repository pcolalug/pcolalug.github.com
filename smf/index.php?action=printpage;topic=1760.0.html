Title: Open Office + Roaming Profiles
Post by: scottstancil on July 08, 2004, 09:03:10 pm
This is not any different than LTSP or many other centralized computing resource schemes that have been around for many, many years.

The problem is that it requires significant bandwidth, computing resources, and people resources to make this work consistently and reliably.  Centralized computing is not the holy grail that it may seem.

In the first age of computing from the 60's to the 70's, mainframes ruled the day and many techies enjoyed their status as stewards of the computing kingdom.  They required non-technical personnel to submit requests for job runs.  It was very expensive.

From the 70's to the mid-to-late 80's computing moved to the desktop and users rejoiced at the ability to run their own reports; however, it still took fairly technically inclined individuals to use it effectively.

From the late 80's-forward, little networked islands developed where end users could run their applications locally on their systems and department computing was formed.  

Now, it seems like bright, technically gifted individuals are trying to force the end users to relinquish control on the data that they may take for granted.  Centralized computing puts the power in the techies hands, while decentralized computing puts it in end user hands.

Office, either, is the crux of this complex issue.  Do I license MS Office with Word, Excel, Powerpoint, Access, and Outlook -- applications that my staff is likely to be highly effective using and administrating?  Or do I go with Open Office -- totally retrain my technical, managerial, or administrative staff for rollout?  Do I go with the known or unknown?  I have to say that it will have very little to do with roaming profiles for many corporations.  Not all corporations are the poster children for totally integrated computing.  Many are simply islands of technology.

I cut this a bit short for brevity's sake, but my point is this idea has been around for a long time.

Thank you for reading.

Scott
