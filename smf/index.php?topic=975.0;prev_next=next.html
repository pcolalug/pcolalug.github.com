Quote from: bosco
Did anyone make mention of the continued dumping of SCO stock by it's higher ups? I thought that was amusing.


I don't believe anyone mentioned it specifically, no.  But what the SCO execs and employees are doing with their stock is perfectly legal -- they're exercising a stock option to automatically sell X number of shares when the share price reaches a certain pre-set point.  It may be morally questionable (amongst other things), but the SEC can't really step in and do anything about it.

Quote from: bosco
Also, worse comes to wose, wouldn't the OSS community just be able to find the offending lines of code, change them and possibly if forced to called it something else? Minux?


Except SCO is unwilling to reveal just what the offending lines of code actually are. Therefore, the OSS community doesn't know what parts are infringing (and thus need to be removed/replaced).  SCO has offered to make the offending lines of code known, but a non-disclosure agreement must be signed first -- the problem with this is, once you've seen the code, you can't tell anyone what you've specifically seen, etc.

Let me make an analogy:

There's something in your house that belongs to me.  I'm not going to tell you what the  object is, exactly, but you owe me money if you wish to continue living inside your house.     Even after you pay me, I'm still not going to tell you what the object is.  Of course, you can pay me even more money and sign an agreement, and I'll be happy to tell you what the object in question is.  But the terms of the agreement state that you cannot remove the object from your house, nor can you can tell anyone else what the object is, much less allow them to remove it.

See how asinine that sounds?
