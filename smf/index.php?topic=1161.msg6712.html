Chuck, Here is some discussion and possible help to your registration problem.

One, you have told it that you have already registered or to never register, right? That should take care of it. If not, then I would assume that you need to move to step two.

Double click the setup file in the OOo download file (after unzipping/tarring). Tell it to repair your installation. I assume that you are using the Linux version? I believe that repair will be your only option. Go through the repair (I don't have much hope of this working, but it is the next easiest thing to do).

If you are using the Windows version, you should be able to do an uninstall from the setup file. Then do a clean install over it. There are two options on the uninstall, you can delete everything (which will take out your data files that are in the installation directory) or just uninstall the program. I would do a complete uninstall. Then reinstall and see if that takes care of you. In Linux, just delete the installation directory (unless you installed using an RPM, then go through the uninstallation using RPM). Then reinstall.

As a last resort, here is a config file that may be the problem. I use this for last, cause I always hate doing stuff like this. It scares me that this could cause other problems. You could try this earlier if you think it might help. In you common.xcs file in the install directory change:
Code:

<Registration>
<ReminderDate cfg:type="string" xsi:null="true" />
<RequestDialog cfg:type="int">0</RequestDialog>
</Registration>

I just picked up the March 2004 issue of Linux Journal. It has a 2 articles about OOo. One is how to make presentations. The other is entitled Eleven Tips for Moving to OpenOffice.org.

I don't want to plagarize and copy the whole article, but I did want to give the 11 points and a brief explaination. You still would want to get the mag to get the full article.

Keep in mind this article is written with the corporate environment in mind.

1. Don't expect features to be missing.
If there is a feature you need, then look for it. Don't just assume that it is missing. It may be implemented in a different way. You can probably look in the the OOo forums mentioned above and get the information you seek.

2. Don't expect features to be in the same place.
If you don't find what you are looking for, keep looking. Assuming that OOo is exactly like MS Office will make it difficult for you to migrate to it. The features are there, just in different places. If you assume that they are in the same place, then you are assuming that Microsoft has the best UI known to man.

3. Don't expect to need training.
For basic features, you can be up and running in a matter of minutes. If you will spend some time honestly trying, you can get it to do most of the advanced features you need within a short period of time.

4. Don't rely on import/export features for exchanging files.
"Why? For one thing writing filters is an intensive and unglamorous job... For free software developers, more interesting projects always are available. Besides, the people who need filters mostly are not developers, so developers are less likely to see the need for them."

Reverse engineering is time consuming and, potentially, legally risky. On top of that, many versions of Microsoft's products are not compatible with other versions of themselves. Why would you expect anyone else to be able to do better?

Though the author has said this, he admits that OOo's MS import/export filters are some of the best he has seen.

Make it a standard in your company, and you will eliminate much of the need to exchange incompatible files. Also, learn to fall in love with exporting to PDF which is native to OOo 1.1.

5. Make a list of how to do the basic functions that you need.
One of the best suggestions I have seen. Get your team together and list the top 20 items that needs to be done. Then have someone figure this out for everyone. Write out note cards that can be distributed with instructions on how to do it. Then as you retire some cards from use, add more complicated tasks to the cheat sheets.

My wife and I do this just among ourselves. She has become the master of certain tasks, while I have wrestled down others. Now if I need help with the spreadsheet, my beautiful and capable office partner can give me some pointers. And vice versa for other tasks that I can handle.

6. Use the available help.
I have to agree with the author, the help files previous to 1.1 were awful. But give them a try now. You probably will be able to find what you need.

7. Use the AutoPilot features.
Let the AutoPilot do some pages for you. You might find that there are more features available in OOo than you first thought.

8. Learn to use styles.
Though not helpful as much for most of my work (one page documents) using styles can be hugely benificial in creating larger documents. Especially if you are doing any kind of formatting. Think of styles in OOo like you would think of styles in HTML. (If you are not familiar with styles and CSS, then give google a visit.)

9. Learn to use the navigator.
Like styles, this is helpful for documents larger than a page or two.

One of the strengths of Word Perfect has been the way it handles large and complicated documents. That is why it is popular amongst lawyers and governments. With styles and the Navigator, OOo has pitted itself as a competitor in this market as well.

10. Look for hidden functionality.
There are some things that OOo does better than other products (after all, it does not attempt to copy all the other word processors exactly). Did you know that you can undo or redo several steps at one time in OOo? I didn't. I assumed that it was one step at a time. Use the undo icon as opposed to cntl-z and see what you can do.

There are other features available. The best way to learn new software is to take some time and play with it. Isn't that the way we learned computers to begin with?

11. Take time before making a decision.
Don't just throw out all of your other word processors. Take time to make the transition. Then allow yourself enough time to make an honest assesment. If you have been using another word processor for 7 years (as I had), then of course you know it very well. You won't be able to work in OOo as efficiently as you did with your other office suite. But you also did not work as efficiently when you started as you do now. I still have my old office suite (Lotus SmartSuite) handy in case I need to do something quick (making labels right the first time without having to think too much), but when I have time, I try to learn how to do the task in OOo.

I enjoyed the article and thought I would give you a heads up to pick up the issue before it is gone from the shelves.
