Good points, everyone (except for the uncomfortable trash talking...). Anyway, I think that, in this case anyway, having Windows and Mac and other platforms in this discussion is very relevent. The whole "debate" is "How do we get the kind of commercial game support that Windows (and even OSX, for crissakes) has"?

My (other) 2 cents:
1. Loki's demise was as much due to pathetic and even criminal management as it was to market share. Had they been able to hold the course and be a bit more conservative, I think they'd be doing quite well today. This is, of course, pure speculation.

2. There are TONS of OpenSource games. There are several decent and even commercial grade game engines available for free or low cost that run cross-platform (one of my personal favorites is Torque).

3. Even with (2), most ambitious OSS game projects never come to fruition, because, well, building games is hard.

4. Like steve always says (in his own, lovable way), vote with your dollars. If a game comes out with Linux support, buy it. If a game comes out promising Linux support, don't buy it until you have the native binary available (this is why I didn't buy Doom3 when it came out).

5. Send a thoughtful, polite email to the publisher of your next anticipated title and ask for alternate OS support.

6. Buy a PlayStation and/or an XBox. I used to snicker at console gamers too. I dare you to play Star Wars Battlefront (think BattleField 1942 but in a galaxy far, far away) and not love it. You may say, "but rburt3, how will this help the commercial Linux game market?" Well, the game industry is in a much tighter market these days, and PC gaming is taking the lions-share of the hit (consoles always out-perform PC games in terms of sales). So, with more "traditional" gamer's dollars going to consoles, the PC crowd may just look for alternative platforms to market to. Sure, we're a small minority of desktop users, but that percentage looks more significant when your overall market share is dwindling. We are also, I think, a great market in which a few small, independent developers could make a decent living from (this is how PC gaming got started, doncha know). We are the hobbiests and enthusiests that are likely to buy into that. Besides, rumor is that the next PlayStation will be a Linux gaming machine anyway...

7. Support hobby OSS games. I know, not everyone is a programmer, but there is a mountain of documentation and support stuff to do for every OSS project out there (even games). Folks who write good documentation are as reviered and respected as the uber hackers in this community (just not as publicly).

8. I think warez are probably less rampant with dedicated Linux users than other platforms. Sure, we expect our infrastructure-type stuff to be free (OS, compiler, libs and drivers), but most of the folks I know gladly shell out the money for good, well-supported software. If you don't, you're slime plain and simple. Not because "programmers gotta eat', but because there are alternatives in most cases that are legally and morrally free, and pirating software is stealing no matter how you justify it. I can't afford a new car, and I need a car to get to and from work. I don't steal the latest greatest car, I use the one I have and learn how to fix it or pay to have it fixed.
