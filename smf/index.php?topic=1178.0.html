THE AWESOME POWER OF DUBYA

Covering one's tracks with the Linux PGP “wipe” command.

By Chuck Steele, Pensacola LUG

20 November 2003

INTRODUCTION:

In early 1979 militant Iranian students seized the U.S. Embassy in Theran. Among other things found during a joyful looting spree were burn bags of shredded documents that had not made it to the incinerator. The students, apparently with a whole lot of free time on their hands, painstakenly put back together many of the shredded documents.

Some of the documents detailed the skullduggery between the CIA and the ousted regime's secret police, SAVAK. At the very least the documents were a source of embarassment to the U.S.

Computer forensic wizards with lots of free time on their hands and clients with deep pockets and access to your hard drive or other storage media can access files you have deleted but have yet to be overwritten with other data. Worse, they can read flat files that may detail your personal information (Example: I get monthly bank statements online in PDF format --- more on this later.).

DUBYA

Linux PGP users have a powerful anti-forensics tool at their disposal, the PGP -w command (There is no equivalent command with Gnu Privacy Guard program yet.). When issued, the command randomizes the contents of a file and then deletes it. When you type:

pgp -w evidence.txt

You get a nice message back, “File evidence.txt wiped and deleted.” A forensic ace may find evidence.txt before it is overwritten but its contents would be a gobbledygook of random bits.

The -w command is useful for general house cleaning when used alone.

More commonly it is used with other PGP commands. Lets say I want to scramble a message, conspiracy.txt, to my friend Mike but do not want to leave the original document laying around. I'll type:

pgp -ew conspiracy.txt Mike

And get a message back, “Pretty Good Privacy(tm) Version 6.5.8

(c) 1999 Network Associates Inc.

Uses the RSAREF(tm) Toolkit, which is copyright RSA Data Security, Inc.

Export of this software may be restricted by the U.S. government.

Recipients' public key(s) will be used to encrypt.

Key for user ID: Mike Mypal <mike@hisisp.com>

2048-bit RSA key, Key ID 0x073D4F71, created 2002/02/17

Ciphertext file: conspiracy.txt.pgp

File conspiracy.txt wiped and deleted.”

Neato!

The conventional encryption feature of PGP for Linux (The -c command.) is generally used to encrypt files on your own computer's hard drive or other storage media (Example: I like to park some encrypted files on a Flash memory card.). Here comes a conditional “ALWAYS.” Once you are confident that you know how to unscramble a PGP encrypted file ALWAYS use the wipe command in conjuction with the -ccommand. Lets do it with my last bank statement:

pgp -cw october.pdf

Lets say I have been in my /home/user/money directory and I type ls. I'll see october.pdf.pgp and no sign of the original file. IF YOU ARE GOING TO BOTHER ENCRYPTING A FILE FOR YOURSELF THERE IS NO SENSE IN LEAVING THE ORIGINAL FILE LAYING AROUND.

Also, it is a good idea to wipe once scrambled files from other people once you are finished with them.

CONCLUSION

You have shredded you paper bank and credit card statements. You have taken the shredded paper out to the rusty old barbecue and burned it. You have let the ashes cool and blasted them with a garden hose. Let the nosy people reassemble the molecules.

With a little effort and a consistent approach you can do the equivalent to correspondence with others and to personal files you regard as worth protecting.
