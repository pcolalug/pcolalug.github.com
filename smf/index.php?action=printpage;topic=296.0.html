Title: Why hate?
Post by: imnes on December 10, 2002, 08:32:21 pm
Well, the reason's you love debian are most likely the same reason's most of us don't.

Package System.  It's very powerful.  Very robust.  And damned intimidating to use at first.  Of course I guess anyone who's fired up the command line and seen the output of rpm would say the same about it.

Old software.  Debian (at least from my limited coverage) seems to have old software with it.  That usually translates to "stable" software which some people like, but some others just see (KDE 2.2.2) when most distros starting using KDE 3.0.0alpha and feel like they've gone back in time or something  :)

No pretty installer.  That's not too big a deal.  How often do you plan to look at the installer anyway?  I don't have a problem using a text based installer, but perhaps that's because it was a long time in MS-DOS and CP/M land before I finally had a computer that could run Windows 3.11/95.

I guess it also has to do with what you're used to.  I'm used to RedHat.  The RPM installer, the /etc/rc.d/ scripts, the filesystem layout.  I know what I'm doing and can figure things out simple.  If I jump into Debian (or slackware or anything else that's different) I have to hitup the internet to figure things out.  Like "How do I configure my ADSL modem?"  Totally different in each distro, but easier for me to hack my way through it in something I"m used to using.

I guess that's pretty much the same stuff stated about using Debian over Redhat/MDK.  Debian sets up a certain way, and you're used to it.  You can find config files, and know how to interact with the scripts.  

I do tend to stick with what I'm used to though.  I use VI almost exclusively.  Emacs is cool, but I can't remember all the keystrokes.  And when I'm used to typing (esc):wq to save my text changes and exit, I find myself trying to suse them in other editors and taking a moment to figure out why it didn't respond (like pico/nano I think it uses CTRL-X or something to exit), and emacs with it's C-x C-s, C-s C-c or whatever the combination is.  

I'm tired from a long night of school.  Sorry if this isn't making total sense right now ;)

I have to admit, the first time I tried to use the package management system, I was intimidated. I hadn't used a package manager before because I had been used to ./configure; make; make install. That was simple to me. Sometimes I had to set some options on the ./configure, but beyond that, I downloaded what I needed and went.

However, it didn't take me long to figure out dselect. Now they have aptitude which has a better ui. If you know the package you are looking for, apt-cache <name> is very simple. It gives you a listing of the available packages that match your search and then you do apt-get install <pkg name>.

I still don't know how to install an rpm from command line and not all of the gui rpm managers are top notch. I will have packages that will make one manager crap out and work with another...so I have three on this laptop running Mandrake...

As far as old software, I agree. I hesitate to use SID, which is considered unstable yet still far behind the bleeding edge. I think the major issue here is the nature of the binaries, interaction with varying systems and the complicated packaging. Has anyone actually tried to put together a .deb? I looked into it once and there is a lot of crap that goes into it. I'm surprised so many people maintain them for the hassel they are.

I also understand the whole thing of using what you are used to. I personally like to try other distros, even flavors of unix, just to see what is different and to see what works.  Most everything I have played with has done one or two things "right", but no one has done it all "right". I guess I'm just either waiting for someone else to do it, or for me to get off my lazy arse and finally make that killer distro...but even then, it won't fit everyone's tastes.
