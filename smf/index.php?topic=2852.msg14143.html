Just for future reference, YaST will handle package dependencies for you if you use their repositories. Most distributions these days include dependency checking within their package manager and will go out and get those dependencies for you. Back in 2000 and 2001, I hated RPM based distros because they wouldn't do this and I got spoiled by apt in Debian. At the time, it was one of the only package managers that would do that. Now, Fedora has yum, SUSE has YaST, Gentoo has portage and they all do the same thing in a high level since. You request a package and it installs it and everything that depends on it.

You are right about the wanting to get something to work now, learn the details later. To be honest, that's the way it goes. The thing to do is that when you are climbing that learning curve, you will find other things that will pique your interest. Make note of those and along the way toward those goals of making X, Y and Z work, you'll learn the basics. It doesn't hurt to read HOWTOs and Manpages. There is an art to reading manpages just like there is an art to searching Google. The way I started learning was by having a simple goal: make a linux firewall. In doing the reading on that, I discovered some other cool stuff a linux box could do. I picked one of those topics, and decided to make that work. Along the way to getting that working, I found out about other cool things. Before too long, I was running a small network of linux systems all doing different things. The learning curve can be steep, but the steep part isn't long. Once you get the feel for how linux works, it all flows pretty well from there.
