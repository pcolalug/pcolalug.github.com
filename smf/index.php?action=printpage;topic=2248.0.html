I find Solaris to be very intriguing. Many of the ways Linux does things comes from Solaris. Look at our SystemV style init scripts (save for Slackware's BSD style) and look at what all the different dev file systems are trying to be. Solaris has built in hardware autodetection, something the Linux has yet to really figure out and make standard.

I'm not saying the Solaris is better than Linux or Linux is better than Solaris. I'm just saying this. Each one has its strengths and weaknesses depending on the situation. Use the right tool for the job. Isn't that what choice, which everyone in the F/OSS community screams about?

Title: Is Sun subverting Linux from the inside?
Post by: rugmonster on February 04, 2005, 05:09:18 am
Out of date usually means stable. You don't just go for bleeding edge unless you absolutely need it when you are dealing with a production environment. Look how many people still use Redhat 7.3, or those still running the 2.2 kernel. OpenBSD doesn't stay on the leading edge. And Debian, known for being incredibly stable, lags behind several years from the latest versions of all of our favorite programs. While I will accept instability in some circumstances, such as on my desktop, when it comes to a production server or workstation that is going to be hammered, I'll take a few out of date packages if money is riding on it.

Also, if there is a driver for you hardware, no matter x86 or sparc, a boot -r will find it. That is provided there is a driver. x86 support is relatively new, but they are moving into the x86 sector, especially with the new Opteron servers and Athlon64 workstations that are out there. Drivers are one thing, autoconfiguration is another.
