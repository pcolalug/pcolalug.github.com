Title: Forced Ads. Are they worth it?
Post by: steve on December 02, 2002, 09:06:47 am
Well, It looks like there's a change taking place on how web sites are getting you to read their Ads. Since Pop-up blockers seem to be abundant and work well, web sites are moving to forced Ads. This is where a site forces your web browser to display an Advertisment page for a period of time before forwarding it to the page you asked for.

I'm sure there will be something devolped, if not already, to get around this. My question is, should we try to block this type of advertising? Are going to end up cutting off our nose to spite our face in the long run?

Advertising is what keeps a lot of these web sites up and running and if the advertisers think they are fighting a losing battle, they may start pulling their Ads. When this happens, sites will no longer be able to foot the cost of hosting and may end up shutting down.

Personally, I don't mind the new forced Ads. It is usually just one Ad and with todays broadband gives me a chance to take a drink of soda.:) Also, it's a lot less intrusive than 50 pop-ups playing in the background.

So, what do you all think?

Steve

Title: Forced Ads. Are they worth it?
Post by: steve on December 02, 2002, 10:06:51 am
Quote from: rburt3
Well, a really effective measure will be people avoiding sites with this feature when possible. Cramming ads down people's throats never won anyone business, IMHO, so this may hurt, rather than help.


I can understand that when it comes to pop-up advertising, as that is very annoying. However, if advertising went away, then how would web site owners/operators pay for bandwidth, servers, etc? Of course they could be advertising free and charge for access to their site, but I've never seen that work before. The other option is to be advertising free and ask for donations. Of course that normally ends up with enough donations to fund a dial-up connection for the hosting server which would end up running on a 386 computer with a 20MB hard drive and 32MB of memory.

So, IMHO, advertising is a necessary evil. (It's kept televesion free for years) The industry needs to come up with a way of making the visitor look at the Ad without making it too anoying. If the Ad is just integrated into the page, most people will not even notice it. If the Ad pops up behind the page, people will close it without paying attention to it. If the Ad appears in your web browser while on the way to a page in the web site, you'll notice it without closing it. (This is exactly what commercials do on television and they've been proven to work without too much complaining)
So, given the option of sites going away because of no advertising or sites being avoided because of too much intrusive advertising, or sites including an (One) Ad between pages on a site, I'll take the later.

While I see banner Ads as comparable to Magazine Ads and the redirection Ads as comparable to TV commercials. Pop-up Ads are comparable to Telemarketers.

You need to remember that advertising, unlike word of mouth and research, is designed to be subliminal and covert. In other words, people say they don't watch commercials and the commercials don't influence their buying power yet how many people drink Pepsi or Coke rather than the less advertised sodas? Oh, you say you prefer Pepsi or Coke to the other stuff? When was the last time you tried ALL of the other stuff? When was the last time you checked the ingredients? They are all pretty close to the same. That's just one example and whoever is reading this may be the exception to that example.
The point is, whether we conciously pay attention to advertising or not, it does affect our buying decisions. The advertisers have a lot smarter people than you or me studying this and they wouldn't pump all of the big bucks into advertising if it didn't work on the "majority" of the population. Look at the price for 30 seconds of superbowl advertising. Are you saying that it doesn't work?
I personally believe that everyone's puchasing decisions are affected by advertising to some degree. Back to my example Coke and Pepsi wouldn't be so much larger and more well known than Shasta and Crown if it weren't for advertising. We may not realize how much it affects our decision to buy Dial over Irish Spring but it does. Anyone who's taken a course in psychology or studied advertising in college knows it works.

Back to the original topic.. I think the advertisers are still trying to figure out what works best on the internet. It doesn't really matter whether we like it or not. What matters is what will influence the buying power of the consumer. So, in the end it may be the subtle banner ads or it may be the obtrusive pop-ups or something in between.
