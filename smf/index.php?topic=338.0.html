Today, I received another newletter from Mandrake. Here is a portion of it...

Quote from: Mandrake Community Newsletter #72
MandrakeSoft's Future.
Despite the financial challenges of maintaining a fully open
source business model, MandrakeSoft has always followed the Free
Software approach, and will continue to do so. After strenuous efforts
over the past 12 months, MandrakeSoft's business model is more solid
than ever with a number of new announcements and exciting
developments on the horizon. But while the medium and long-term
prospects for the company are very good, we are experiencing short-term
cash-flow difficulties which are impacting the progress of the upcoming
release.....

New Product
----------------------------------------
Multi-Network Firewall has been released.
MandrakeSoft's new Multi-Network Firewall (MNF) is one of the most
feature-rich firewall products currently available on the market.


Okay, notice that they are talking about their "cash flow" problems then go on to talk about their new product, the Multi-Network Firewall. I looked into this "new" product, which turns out to be nothing more than a collection of IPTables, Snort and several other open source packages with a pretty web based front end (modded webmin?). To top all this off, they are making it /all/ available under the GPL and downloadable.  

My problem here is, they also have a "commercial" license available. Here is their short explaination of it...

Quote from: MandrakeSoft
Not only does this free you from several requirements of the GPL license which might be incompatible with your project, it also provides product warranties and access to important services such as support & easy updating.


If the packages they are using are GPLed, how can they claim that their product sold under the "commecial license" is free of the constraints of the GPL. So here we have a product, being very easy to push to businesses, which could make them a fair amount of money. Instead, they try to make businesses think that the "commercial licensed" version is somewhat better because it has support and easy updates and somehow "GPL free". All the while, the downloadable version seems no different, just without support and "licensed under the GPL", like that's a bad thing.

Okay, I'm ranting, but this just seems stupid as a money making ploy. Once again, making it free (as in beer and speech) like their regular distribution. This is apparently not making them enough money, yet they pass up an opportunity with another product. I guess I make too much sense...or I'm not being idealistic or optimistic enough.

I just hope for Mandrake's sake, that the appliance to go along with MNF takes off or else, I see people downloading the "GPL" version and passing up the support that goes with the "commercial" version.

All I can say is this is hurting my view of Mandrake. For me to respect a business, they have to use some smart practices. I guess they will either wise up or go bankrupt which would be a shame considering the quality of their products.

*sigh*

I guess that is smarter business practice, but I don't like it. I guess that is why I don't use those distros.

I am having a very difficult time with this. Call it an internal debate with myself. I can see both sides of this issue. On one side, you have the ideals of free software. On the other, the world of business and the all mighty dollar (yen, mark, euro, etc). They are fundamentally opposing ideas.

My knee jerk reaction to the thought of Redhat, Mandrake and SuSE making their distros unavailable for download is to call foul. It just doesn't seem right to take a bunch of free software, put it together with some pretty config utilities and sell it. It seems only right to give back the finished products to the community which helps develop and test those free packages.

Then I have to think about the time that went into making that distro. Also, the GPL only provides for the necessity of the source being made available, not binaries. Also, not all projects use the GPL. So really, the businesses aren't doing anything wrong if they don't offer free downloads of their product as long as they aren't violating the licenses of the individual packages that make up that product.

I guess this is another reason why I don't like the way business works, but it is a necessary evil. I definitely think it the practices of Xandros and Lycoris go against the spirit of free software, but they aren't doing anything dead wrong. If the other major distros go that path (thank god Debian won't), it won't be popular, but nothing can stop them.

Keep in mind, though, that the individual packages still keep their original licenses, no matter who puts them together. My original post was focused on this. The wording on Mandrake's site made it sound like by buying the "commerical" license, you were somehow freed of the constraints of the GPL. That is just misleading and false advertising. I hope that it doesn't come back to bite them in the ass because I do like Mandrake. They at least seem like they are keeping the ideals of free software in their business model, no matter how impractical a business plan it is...at least in their business.

I think the only way for a company to really make money on free software is to provide some sort of value added service with it. I would give examples, but this has taxed me terribly and don't feel like it. Never before has it taken me an hour to write something this short.

Don't get confused. "OpenOffice.org" and "Star Office" (You have to include the ".org" due to copyright issues with another product.) are different products made by different companies. "Star Office" is a Sun product that Sun owns. "OpenOffice.org" is a Open source community product, not affiliated with Sun anymore, that is coded by the open source cummunity. Sun took an old version of "Star Office" and released the source code to the public domain a couple of years back. That source code has since matured into "OpenOffice.org". "Star Office" belongs soley to Sun and is sold by Sun. It is no longer offered for free. (They did offer a beta version but I think it has been pulled since the latest final version was released.)

http://www.openoffice.org/FAQs/mostfaqs.html#7

Go there to see what was changed from StarOffice 5.2 before the code was GPLed and released to OO.org as well as to find out the major differences between OO.org and StarOffice.

Also, for a while, many people in the OSS movement had major problems with running StarOffice because it was closed source. The folks responsible for Debian had a majorly hard time with it and would never support it. So after much pressure and nashing of teeth, I think it was more of a political move on Sun's part to release the old code for the OSS community to play with and call their own. Good move on them because OO.org does give many props to Sun and it therefore brings more attention to Sun's commercial product StarOffice. Also, if you notice, all versions of StarOffice are going to be based on OO.org, thus cutting down their development costs. For Sun it is a win-win situation. Let the code monkeys they don't pay make OpenOffice.org, then take that code and base the commercial StarOffice off of it. All they have to do is use the LGPL on libraries to interface their closed stuff with the GPLed OO.org stuff and viola! New version. That means they only have to focus on one or two programs rather than six or seven. Since they are aiming at businesses with StarOffice, they don't care if Joe Sixpack can download the version without the database and fonts and clipart that he'll never use. Rather, the businesses want that database and the other extras. Heck, they want the support of Sun and they'll pay for it. Sun is making out like a bandit by letting the people do its work for them and making the community feel good about having something to call their own.
