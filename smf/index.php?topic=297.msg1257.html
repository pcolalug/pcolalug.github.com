this really should have been another topic


My boss and I have discussed this many times in the past and the problem that keeps coming up is that very few bussineses, that we deal with, use just an Office suite and e-mail. Most bussineses including ours use some sort of proprietary application and they are usually Windows based. Here are a a couple of examples:

1. The company I work for is a MS solution provider and programming company for the Govt. Our programmers use the MS Visual Developers suite because all of our clients requirements revolve around Windows. These are clients that have contracted us to write applications for them, not to reccomend an OS. So, we are locked in, in that case.  We also host web sites for clients that require Active X as well as Front Page publishing and some other publisher that uses IIS. Again, locked in.

2. Some of our clients are Law Firms. They require a practice menagement application to handle their clients cases. These apps have the law time lines built into them such as fileing requirements, etc. I haven't seem any Linux based Law Firm Practice managent software. In addition, some of them use terminal services. This would be great in Linux, except that the application being served up, again, is a program called ProLaw. Look it up on the Web. It requires a Windows Server.

3. We also had a client that was in the rental car bussines. They also had software to manage their rental car agency. It tied into a national database for rental agencies. Again, Windows based.

4. Finally, our biggest clients are US Government. They bought heavily into MS years ago and even with all the talk about the government looking at Linux they have yet to take any actions. (At least our clients haven't taken any actions)

5. We also have a fast food franchise client. Again, corporate HQ has a couple of Windows based apps that are required. Stuck again.

Now you may ask, why not just run something like WINE?  IS the time worth the cost? Remember, our companies time is money. When we can install Windows and get the Windows apps up and running without even cracking the quick start guide, we're making money. When we have to setup WINE, tweak it for the App we want to run, test it, and then have no support from the manufacturer for problems with the app, because we aren't running it under Windows, that costs us money.

Finally, hardware. Businesses require more robust hardware than is currently supported under Linux. For instance, Xerox priniter that's the size of a small desk and was just released on the market, does it have Linux drivers for all of it's functions like printing, copying, faxing, scanning, and yes e-mailing, We have a client that has one of these. They are network devices but only use Windows drivers. We can't tell a client that when they need to buy hardware that they need to be extra carefull and make sure it will work under Linux. (Don't say you need to do the same thing with Windows. Face it, Windows is the standard when manufaturers develop drivers and hardware apps.)


So, back to your original question. If I happen to find a small client that only needs to surf the net, e-mail, and do office documents and nothing else, then I would reccomend Linux. (If they ask) The other thing is to not scare a client into thinking they need a major overhaul to get their bussines done. This only scares them off. If they are happily using Windows I will never knock it unless they ask for a recommendation.

Just my 2 cents.

Steve

Ah, but even if the users don't use the feature, see what happens when you take it away from them. We did that in the Govt when we migrated from Lotus cc:Mail to Exchange. Even though Exchage had more features and the users didn't use most of the features in cc:Mail, they complained about the loss of features when it was taken away from them.  Go figure.  

A couple of things about Exchange that people don't understand.

1. A lot of the groupware functionality that isn't used is because it's not setup/pushed/advertised by the administrators. Many administrators don't know about the power Exchange offers you.  
A good example is that you can develop custom forms in it using pretty much drag and drop programming. I developped a whole helpdesk section in our Exchange server within a couple of days keeping us fram having to buy or install a seperate helpdesk app.
Another feature rarely used is public folders. In Exchange, you can setup public folder for groups of users.  They can manage them as well as hide them from other users/groups. We use this extensivle for manageing different departments. We setup a folder hierarchy for the admin department that contains their own shared address book, contact lists, calendar, etc. It works out great.
In our company, we have seperate folders setup for each of our contracts that contains all e-mail, contacts, scheduleing, and documents, etc pertaining to the contract. Then when we get a new person on board and they are working with that contract, we just add them to the folder permissions.

Many companies setup shared directories on thier servers for a lot of this stuff. That can all be integrated into Exchange so it's one stop shopping for everything from personal e-mail to documents on a contract or even stored files. All of this can be stored in Exchange. Most Exchange administrators either, don't know about all of these features or don't know how to impliment them.

This is why Exchange is so powerful as a groupware solution. Again, I'm waiting to see what SuSE comes out with.
