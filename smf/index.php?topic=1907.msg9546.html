Slackware going to drop Gnome?
« Reply #17 on: October 11, 2004, 04:08:30 pm »
sad to say, but it's looking like it will happen....... not sure if it will happen in one of the future releases in the next few years or will just bounce around as an idea for a while until it finally comes time to put slackware on a diet, or if he's just wondering about the response from the gnome group to shape up

I sent Patrick 2 emails and both he's replied to very quickly, and as much as I would like to have slackware with gnome, he does have many valid points....

Quote

I've been ranting in provate emails about wanting to remove it for many
years, but I don't know if I'd have ever made the move to do so.  HOWEVER,
since my feelings were outed on Slashdot and elsewhere I have to say that
I'm very surprised that I'm not getting much opposition to the idea.  I
might actually remove it and leave independant groups to package GNOME for
Slackware.

Thanks.  I'm glad you've enjoyed the GNOME shipped with Slackware, and I'd
agree that Dropline may not be making all the right decisions.  But
there's always room for another group to take the GNOME basis that's in
Slackware and start maintaining it in a minimalistic way.  The fact is,
I'm not happy with the way GNOME is going, and don't want to add things
like DBUS and HAL to Slackware (mark my words, they'll be a major decurity
hole at some point).  So, what to do?  The reality is that having me carry
the torch on GNOME is stealing away from the rest of Slackware.

I'm open to suggestions, but the honest truth is that I need to cut back
to avoid burning out, and following the moving GNOME target has become too
much effort for too little return.  I'd rather not even ship GNOME through
Slackware any longer, and part of that is that I think Slackware hasn't
resisted growth as much as it should have.  I'd like to simplfy things,
and have really started to dislike bundling as much on top of the OS as I
do.  It doesn't all need to be maintained here, or even shipped from here.
The "bundle everything" approach is not a healthy way to go.

Sincerely,

Pat

Quote

I'm not willing to try to manage or hassle an external project.  The
situation with GNOME is not something I'm going to be able to fix.  In as
few words as possible, what needs to happen is:

There need to be fewer GNOME packages.  Sources should be grouped into
larger collections like the KDE tarballs are.

GNOME should not require as many undocumented system changes, like HAL and
DBUS.  KDE doesn't need any of that.

Every GNOME package should be able to use standard configure options.  For
example, every single KDE package may be built and installed with this
simple recipe:

./configure \
  --prefix=/opt/kde \
  --with-xinerama \
  --disable-debug \
  --program-prefix="" \
  --program-suffix="" \
  i686-slackware-linux
make
make install DESTDIR=$PKG

In all candor, I don't ever see these issues being addressed within GNOME,
and honestly feel I would be wasting my time to try to get anything done
about them.  I doubt they would even agree that anything needed to be
done.

I could go on and on about other things that I think are design problems
instead of simply build problems like the above, but that's enough for
now.

I don't know why Slackware needs to bundle everything.  People complain
when Microsoft does this.  Anyway, all I can say is that it's hard to
lead.     Most of the emails so far have been pretty nice, but I
understand that people feel that because it's been supported for a few
years that it needs to remain, and that I should continue to support it.
But I'm like a guy treading water with a couple of buckets tied to my
ankles, and every little package is like a couple more pebbles tossed in
there.  I've long felt that it's all gotten too big, and that trying to
allow that (though whatever means) is not the right approach.  Slackware
badly needs to go on a diet.  Everyone else can bundle in the kitchen
sink, but I think it's not in keeping with Slackware's minimalist
approach, and neither (for that matter) is GNOME.

Take care,

Pat

You know it's funny you mention that, rburt3, because I was JUST thinking that I might try to make Slackware "more accessible" if Gentoo doesn't work out.  Part of me wants to cling to Slackware only because I want to learn it inside and out but Gentoo, in terms of core workings, isn't that different, right?  I mean, there's no "strangeness" regarding the file system (like Fedora) and it's only main difference is extreme portage?


This is one of the things I try constantly to open eyes up to:  variety.  When someone comes over to my house that's never seen Linux before, I show them what's most shocking and different.  I start it up, put it in KDE, and ask 'em to come see.  They ask me what's so "incredibly" different but will at first admit it's pretty cool-looking.  So I show them the pager and the nice, clean interface, but I tell them then that it doesn't have to be that way if they don't want it to.  Naturally they're thinking in terms of themes, so I warp their thinking pretty quickly by closing out KDE, booting up Gnome, and telling them it's still Linux.  Or booting up Fluxbox is a favorite for a shocker.

Bar none, the percentage of people who are fascinated with the possibilities of Linux after such a small demonstration of its capability for adaptation is 100%.  100% out of everyone I've shown Linux to.  That's amazing.
