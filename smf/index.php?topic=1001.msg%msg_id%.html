Since Mandrake is developped and distributed by a company of people working full time on it and depending on it as a source of income, and they do offer Mandrake for sale then yes that is leaching. If Apache was developped and distributed by a company of full time employees depending on it for income and it was available for sale, then I think people should buy it.

The question that needs to be asked is why even bother selling something you're going to give away? Given a choice of buying a product or taking the exact same product for free, most people would take it for free. If putting food on my childrens dinner plates depends on me selling my product, whether I believe in open source or not, I'm going to try to make the money necessary to feed my children. Most "Open Source" project don't care because they are not relying on the product as their source of income. If they are, then they're bigger fools than Mandrake for giving away their product and allowing their families to starve.

Open Source, IMHO, is designed around the contribution/spare time concept. ie., programmers contribute bits of code to a bigger project based on the amount of spare time they have to work on the project. Some of the programmers may work full time on the project only because they have other sources of income whether it be a full time job, a retirment check, or a spouse that's supporting them.

Quote from: Rugmonster
Quote from: steve
I have posted to Mandrake numerous times that I think they should only put the new distro out for download after it's been on store shelves for a couple of weeks.

I'm not sure that would be completely compliant with the GPL.

Also, unless they put some sort of copyright on the actual disk, what would stop people from purchasing the commercial version and hosting it independently?


1. It's called Open "Source" meaning the source code is free, not necessarily the compiled code. The GPL states that you must include the source code with any compiled code you distribute/sell. You can still sell your distribution before making it available for download as long as the GPL software has the source code included. Look at SuSE. This is exactly what they do.

2. A distribution is not inherantly free to ditriibute just because it contains GPL code. All the GPL license requires is that the source code for the GPL portion be included with the distribution. You can still write a compiled and copyrighted install program, make it a part of the distribution and not allow the free downloading or redistribution of that distribution.
