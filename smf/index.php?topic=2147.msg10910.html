Just pour a half pint of 10W-40 in there every month or 10,000 web pages (whichever comes first).

Outside of that there are a couple of things you can do. The fan idea would probably work, and a little probing around with a multimeter (carefully) would probably give you a good idea as to where to tap in. But, probably the easiest, and best thing is to have nothing on the modem that is also generating heat. Having above and below the modem open will cut down on the heat. All those holes were designed for a reason.

Might sound odd but try reversing them.  If you've got the wireless router on top of the modem and the modem's got an issue try it the other way around...the router might deal with the heat better as Linksys products seem to be made to be stacked.


Another alternative to the fan idea (good idea, I might add, I even added in a CPU fan into my car to cool down my stereo on hot days, pretty slick really) is to come up with a new method of stacking them.  Such as, if you can get some spare wood you can fashion up a miniature shelf so they're at least four inches apart.

friend of mine did this with his linksys WRT54G after he boosted the output power by a large amount and it started to run hot, he just drilled out the holes on the bottom, cut a 70x70mm hole in the top and mounted one of the 70mm TMD(tip magnetic drive fan, no electric motor in the middle) that moved i believe about 40cfm and found a 12v dc power brick that matched the amount of current he needed for the fan
http://www.newegg.com/app/ViewProductDesc.asp?description=35-106-601&depa=0
I wasn't able to find a TMD fan by itself, but i found the cheapest heatsink that came with the fan lol

http://www.frozencpu.com/fan-81.html
http://www.frozencpu.com/fan-80.html

another option is find one of them crappy <80w powersupplies made for like p2 celeron budget machines that is a microsized atx powersupply and use it for a power source heh

I think Steve has a valid point, though I would agree that it is not a problem. If I remember my electronics well enough (I can look this up if we need to debate it), if you have a power supply with the proper voltage, but higher amperage, then you could do this safely. The higher amperage would take care of the extra drain that the fan would require. But not be damaging to the unit like higher voltage would be (though there is an amazing tolerance in these components for higher or lower voltage. But the device is made to run at a particular voltage to maintain proper timing.)

In my feeble mind this is borne out by my truck. The amperage of the battery is much more than my dome lamp needs to operate. But yet the extra amperage does not destroy the lamp with too much current.

I would think that finding yourself a proper voltage power supply with a higher amperage will eliminate all concerns. Though, I am not concerned with the current set-up (mainly because it ain't mine to worry about).
