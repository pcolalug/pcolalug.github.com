OK. First off for an ISP to prevent you from sending mail though an outside e-mail server using smtp (Outlook Express) would be senseless. I have never run across this and I've used at least 5 different ISPs. I currently use Cox as my ISP but use my own domain hosted by my company to send my e-mail through. Cox does not block the smtp port (IP Port 25).

You need to make sure when you set up your e-mail accounts in Outlook Express that you match the sending smtp server with the receiving account. Otherwise you create a relay situation which most e-mail servers will not allow. What happens in a relay situation is that you are sending e-mail from user@cox.net through the e-mail server at mydomain.com. Most email servers will not allow this. That's why the account receving server and sending server must match. Believe me, I deal with this all day.

However, make sure you set your default e-mail account to whichever one you want to use. This makes it easier in the long run when you are forwarding mail or creating new mail.

OK. I went back and read through the thread again. hitechwizard is right. Cox has probably shutdown the relay on their mail server. If you'r sending an e-mail and the "From" address is not the same as the server your sending it through, it should not be able to be sent. In other words if my from address says "steve@mydomain.com" then I must send it though the mail server at mydomain.com. If the email from address read steve@cox.net then I must send it through the cox.net mail servers. I can not send an email with the from address of steve@mydomain.com through the cox.net servers. This would be relaying and is bad.

In order to make things easier, you can go into your account settings for each of your mail accounts and change the "Reply To" address to your prefered mail address. This way, eventually, most email will come to that address meaning most email you reply to will also go out that server. Also, remember to make your "Default mail account" the one you prefer. This is what's used when you originate email.

Actually, as indicated by http://support.cox.net/custsup/email/email_info.shtml , that isn't the case.  The problem is that COX has blocked the ability for users of its service to send e-mail through 3rd party outbound servers.

Farther down, it mentions that you can send e-mail that appears to be from (ie, the from or reply-to headers) any valid domain.  This means that although you can send e-mail using an email address such as user@whatever.org, you must still send it through the appropriate COX smtp server.  The smtp server settings for this can be found at http://support.cox.net/custsup/email/POP_SMTP_settings.shtml .

They also mention, further down:

"Q: I pay for my own domain name so I can send personal email through that server. Why are you blocking this legitimate use?
A: We understand that some customers have vanity domain names and this action should not keep you from using your personal domain name. To ensure your ability to send email from your personal domain, your mail client must be set to a Cox outgoing mail server. This change in settings will be transparent to email recipients, and will not impact your ability to receive email. If you have a business requirement to bypass the Cox mail servers, please contact Cox Business Services for additional options."

David -- you aren't the only one experiencing this...it goes for all residential cox customers.

OK. I did a little research via Google News Groups and found that Michael is right. Cox is blocking outgoing SMTP connections. They are doing this to get control of the Spammers on their network.

Mine must be working because my company is on Cox's network.

Anyway, you need to just set your smtp server to Cox's mail server. Set your "Reply-To" address to your private mail server. This will allow you to still recevie mail through your private domain and all replies to mail you send should go there. You will only be using Cox's server to send your outgoing mail.

Think of it as sending mail though a local post office but the return address on all of your mail is somewhere else. It has no affect on your availability to use a seperate mail server for receiving mail.

I prefer to use my personal domain to receive mail because it's not ISP dependant. That way I can change ISPs anytime I want without any impact on my email address.
