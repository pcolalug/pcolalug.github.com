Hi, was checking out the forums and catching up a bit.  The new forums look fantastic BTW.

As quite a few of you know, I converted to Mac almost 3 years ago.  I suspect that market share percentage is about the same, but the markets are different.  You just have to look back at Loki's mishandling to understand the differences.  In several of the pundit's takes on Loki's failings, the Linux community was too cheap to buy them was prevalent.  I always thought that was bull because I bought just about every Loki game that came out -- more games for Linux than for my Mac.  What I remember back then is how it was painful to get it running sometimes.  I still see that to some extent.  I am almost exclusively a Linux server person now and even now couldn't recommend Linux to most of my friends.

When I recommended Linux to a local school I am helping, there was a lot of blinking.  On the server side, they were luke warm -- on the client side, frosty.  Frosty because of ignorance of projects like LTSP or the fact they could simply not spend money, no.  Six months later, still no Linux in house, even after offering to take care of the server's patches and emergencies for next to nothing.

I understand a lot has changed in many distros to make them easier one way or the other, but as gaming platforms for this gamer, there is still a lot to be desired.  Heck, I even got tired of putting money in my Windows or Mac machines in favor of a XBox360.  It's easier.  (Just like PS 3 or Wii will be easier.)  Put the DVD in.  Wait a few seconds.  Play. Save game.  Turn off.  Heck it was easier to surround sound with this console than it was to do so with my PC.  And if you haven't played Halo or Oblivion on an XBox, you don't know what you are missing.

The point...right I have one.  I don't think you are going to see more games coming your way until it just works no matter what, no matter what distro you are using, packaged up the same for all distros.  And note that the games that you quote are simply rehashes of existing technology from the same old companies.  I for one am not looking a gift horse in the mouth -- the community should be grated for id's and Epic's engines and their dedication to running clients on Linux.

That said, are there any initiatives to get the myriad of distro's to finally unify libraries related to game developement in lockstep or provide a package management interface wrapper for developers that isn't to painful to learn?

7  Distributions / Other Operating Systems / What will Apple Intel do to Linux on: June 09, 2005, 09:17:07 pm
I think it is early to guess what the move will do.  Switching to Intel has become a necessity because the PowerPC is just not going to keep up with Intel going forward.  If they don't make the switch, Apple could simply lose its ability to compete at all.

It isn't about taking market share from Microsoft, if market share is considered at all; it's about taking market share from everyone.

OS Xi will only run on Apple produced hardware.  I understand the Windows will run on the current test hardware available from Apple because it is fairly standard.

I find it interesting that two years ago, Steve Jobs touted the PowerPC, especially Altivec and touted that the PPC was much faster than Intel's processors.  For some things, it was and for other things it simply was not.

Ruggy, I am disappointed in you.  Why would anyone buy an Apple over building your own and running Linux?  You answer your own question, OS X.  Emulation will be easier as many developers have weighed in on because of Endian issues.  We could see a much better version of VirtualPC or even VMWare.

Getting back to why it could pull Linux users.  In my case, I wanted a UNIX based system with a decent user interface, MS Windows, and decent games.  Going forward, I am hopeful that Intel's ability to supply Apple with not just quality M chips, but a LOT OF THEM, allowing Apple to drop prices, increase speed, and reduce power consumption.  Not all Linux users and/or developers want to build their own boxes or recompile something to get sound working on their laptops new BIOS-less laptops.

I also don't think that this move will kill Linux by any stretch of the imagination.  There are folks like yourself that won't or don't want to leave it.  Those that don't understand why folks like myself pay Apple for their OS.  (Check out opendarwin.org when you get a free moment.)  Those that love to tweak that last little thing that you just can't do under OS X.

For example, I tried OS X Server on a dual G5 for about 4-5 months in my consulting biz.  At the end of the journey, I put OS X on the dual G5 and use it for graphics and development work, ordered a Dell Server and installed RHEL4.  Dual G5+OSX Server?  Close to $3k.  Single CPU Dell server that more than handles the load at this point plus RHEL 4? Close to $500 and is much more flexible.

Anyways, just my two cents.  Have fun folks.

Dear Valued Go Daddy Customer,

Effective November 12, 2004, the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names
and Numbers (ICANN) will institute a new transfer policy for all
accredited domain name service providers.

The previous ICANN policy allowed us to deny requests to transfer your
domain names to another registrar unless you explicitly confirmed to us
your intent to transfer. The new ICANN policy removes that protection.
Starting November 12, when we receive a request to transfer your domain
name to a new registrar, we will still attempt to contact you to confirm
that you authorized the request. However, if you do not respond, or are
not able to respond within 5 days, your domain name WILL be transferred.

With this change in mind, we are recommending that all Go Daddy domain
customers visit GoDaddy.com and "lock" their domain name(s). Locking your
domain(s) is free and prevents unauthorized changes to contacts and name
servers. Most importantly, it prevents the domain name from being
transferred to another registrar without your knowledge. You may lock or
unlock your domain at any time.

To lock your domain names:

   Go to: https://www.godaddy.com/?isc=gdficgdout
   Click My Account and enter your username or customer number and password.
Click Manage Domains.
   Select the domain name(s) that you wish to lock.
   Click Set Locking. In the right side pane, select "Lock" and save your
changes.

After your changes have been submitted, there will be a short duration
while your request is pending update. During this period, details of the
domain(s) will be temporarily unavailable.

If you have any questions please email us at support@supportwebsite.com or
call our customer support line at (480) 505-8899.
