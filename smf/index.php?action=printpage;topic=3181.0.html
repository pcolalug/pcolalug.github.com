Title: Re: Is a firewall necessary for Linux?
Post by: crowe on June 18, 2007, 06:52:05 pm
Assuming that things haven't changed with the most recent release(s), an install of Ubuntu should have no public services running by default.  Installing and configuring a firewall*, such as Firestarter or Lokkit is still a good idea, in the event that you (either knowingly or otherwise) should ever install a server program that opens up a port by default.








* Actually, you already have a firewall installed -- iptables.  Firestarter, Lokkit, and the like are just GUI frontends that allow you to easily configure iptables, which by default comes with no pre-set configuration.

I made an assumption that linuxnewbie would be putting it directly on the Internet. I said the very same thing in a response to him/her last night.

I think the prevalence of Windows and the attempts to make up for its shortcomings without explaining what firewalls are for has given people a false sense of security as long as they are running an app called a firewall, no matter what, like antivirus and antispyware. Marketing for marketing sake has put the term firewall in most computer user's vocabulary, but I would say most don't realize exactly what they are blocking/allowing or why you would even want to do that besides the obvious "to keep things out." Granted, NAT is not a firewall, but most home routers at least do simple stateful packet inspection, and that is enough for most people and most circumstances. The mindset that every system needs a firewall for security has spread thanks to Microsoft following XP SP2, Norton/Symantec, McAfee, et al. They, like always, wanted to make a buck and it was simply easier to market firewalls to everyone and skip the consumer education part. Most people haven't looked into it more, so they have bought into the idea, quite literally.
