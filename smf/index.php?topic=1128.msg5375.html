Besides installed / removing apps I don't expect end-users to be 'administering' their own system much at all.  Some things can be a pain in the but under both systems - Printers come to mind.  It used to be the case that printers just worked under Windows.  But as soon as you get something newer / exotic everything falls apart  Ever try to install a recent USB Xerox (or HP or Epson) printer / scanner in windows?  Forget that it usually doesn't work on the first (or second try), I love the warning labels on the printer, the driver CD, and on-screen when you put in the CD.  "DO NOT PLUG UP YOUR PRINTER UNTIL AFTER YOU HAVE INSTALLED THE SOFTWARE!"  Well that's kind of odd, isn't windows supposed to be plug and play?  I open the pretty box my printer came in, plug in the USB cable, and I can print?  Oh well...  It was a nice concept  Maybe this is why I run a 10 year old HP LaserJet 3 that does just work everywhere....

As far as Mac / OSX goes.  I don't know yet.  I'm on the look-out for a good deal (unless I talk myself into buying a new apple notebook).  When I get one I'll be sure to share my experiences.

On another topic - application development - I guess MS-Windows toolkits were better than anything else out there.  I was speaking with an professor at school yesterday, he used to do GUI development on Mac's around 1990.  He was explaining how low-level the coding was, each application was responsible for drawing everything itself, when your app window got resized, you were responsible for recalculating the scrollbar size / position and redrawing it, and everything else.  That sounds like a pain!
