I've had fairly good luck with both manufacturers. From my experience, the nvidia chipsets are typically more stable than the ATIs and more game manufacturers gear their games toward nvidia chipsets.

When nvidia makes a chipset, they release a sample card and to make a card based off of that chipset, they must meet strict specifications. No board is seriously worse than another in any way other than quality of manufacturing. Some research on Tom's Hardware should give you a good idea of what's available and the overall quality/performance advantage per chipset.

nvidia releases their standard driver and all nvidia cards since the Riva TNT use those unified Detonator drivers, and they are made available for Win32, Linux and FreeBSD.

Performance wise, from what I understand, it is pretty close depending on who you talk to. I have a friend who has a Radeon 9800 and loves it, except he gets some strange artifacts in StarWars Galaxies. Other than that it works great. The GeForce FX series is good, but I have heard of problems with the high end versions. If you hold out for a few weeks, you will probably see card prices drop as the spring rolls around and the next generation of cards is readied for release.

I don't have any (recent) experience with ATI, but have had several nVidia's in the last 2-3 years.  Without exception, the nVidia's have performed flawlessly, both in Windoze and Linux.  I would definitely go with nVidia.

Quote from: dpeach
nVidia cards that are not actually made by nVidia, are they any good? Do they use actual nVidia drivers, or will I have to depend on the company to supply the drivers?


Yes, they are fine.  I currently have a GeForce4 Ti 4200 made by PNY that has been perfect.  They use the actual nVidia drivers (the Linux version of which is very easy to install).

Quote from: dpeach
Any particular card you would recommend? I would like to spend $100 or less.


I think the (fairly new) nVidia GeForce FX series are decent, and the FX5200 should be well under $100.  There are a couple of higher models (5600,5700?) that might be a little more expensive.  If you can still find a GeForce4 Ti 4200 on the shelf, it is a great card and should be around that price too.  I don't actually have any first-hand knowledge of the FX series, and I "think" the FX5200 may actually be outperformed by a GF4 Ti 4200.  I do know that the GeForce series with an "MX" in the model name are lesser cards and I would avoid them.

HTH and let us know what you get...

I ended up with a Ti 4200 128MB RAM. I looked for a Gainward, but the only one I could find (on eBay) was a 64MB.

I did some reading on benchmarks. First, I must say that some people get way too pickey.

The 64 MB actually outperforms the 128 on about 80% of the tests I read. The 64 has a faster clock speed. The difference was very small, but the 64 was faster most of the time. But where the 128 really waxed the 64 was in the Comanche 4 game. The explanation was that the extra RAM helps with the pixel and vertex shaders. I am sure that means something to someone. The page did say that having that extra RAM will help with future games, though it was not that big of a deal right now. It also helps at higher resolutions.

There was basically no price difference for the extra RAM, so that I what I ended up with.

The 4200 has reports of being able to be overclocked to perform just like a 4600. The benchmarks were amazing. I don't do overclocking, but I will keep this bit of information in the back of my head if I decide the card is not performing well a couple of years from now.

Thanks for your input.
