Some other things you guys might find useful but may not be complete yet:
 
Gnome has been integrating system configuration tools into the desktop.  Gnome provides the front-end GUI and the distribution's write their custom back-end to interface it to their config files.  The ultimate goal is that a user can step up to any system (Solaris, any Linux disro, *BSD, Mac, etc.) running Gnome and configure their systems with the same familiar tools.  

Evolution is integrated fairly well into Gnome now.  You can click on the clock, select a date and Evolution will start up so you can enter Events / Reminders into the calendar system.  Even with evolution not running you will get your reminders and alerts pop-up when they should.

Quote from: imnes
How do you like that Dvorak keyboard, is it really any better than qwerty?  I was considering trying one out but was told since I use computers at home, school, and work that I should stick with qwerty.

Absolutely better! But, that comes with a disclaimer that I am not quoting research and studies. Just my personal opinion. I have been using it regularly for several months.

Yes, it can be difficult to make the switch. My biggest problem is going back to qwerty. Since I learned to touch type so many years ago and my qwerty skills were excellent (70 WPM range) I really can not tell you where the keys on the keyboard are. If you stuck a gun to my head I could not tell you the layout. But stick a keyboard in my hand and I could bang out any sentence you want. That would be a good thing in most cases. But, now that I am typing with Dvorak, I have made that layout my habit, and I am now at a total loss for the qwerty keyboard.

When I have to use qwerty though I can do alright if the letters are on the keys. It takes me a couple of minutes of typing and I can switch back to qwerty mode without too much effort. But on my main system, I have pulled all the key caps off my keyboard and moved them around to the Dvorak layout while I was learning. That means that my main system no longer has the letters in the qwerty spots. If I am stuck typing qwerty on this keyboard, it really is a hunt and peck system for me.

That said, I don't think having to use qwerty is all that bad if you are a Dvorak typist. But for me it takes looking at the keys for a minute or two until I switch modes. Then I can get back to my old speed skills within just a few minutes.

If you are at the same workstation at work all the time, then it is not a problem to set up the two different keyboards and just switch it back (in Windows it is a simple two key stroke) to normal when someone else needs to use your system.

It does take effort to switch, but I would think that those of you who work in the computer field would have the most to gain from the switch. There are all kinds of comments made about Dvorak on the net. But the two most impressive to me are that you can type over 5000 English words from the Dvorak home row. I cannot find the number, but it is only something like 30 English words can be typed from the Qwerty home row. The second fact is concerning the distances your fingers travel while typing with the two layouts. It is multiple times more distance with qwerty. 70% of all typing takes place on the Dvorak home row.

Here is a page that gives a quick run down.
http://www.dvorak-keyboard.com/

Switching was not easy. It took some effort. My speed is only about half of what it was before. But every time I have an IM chat with someone, or ICQ, I feel myself being pushed to make my speed increase. I have done little speed increasing activities since moving to Dvorak. With qwerty, I was always working on my speed. Right now with Dvorak I am trying to get my accuracy up before shooting my speed through the roof.

Personal goal is to be able to type consistently over 100 WPM. Just one of those things I would like to be able to do before I die.
