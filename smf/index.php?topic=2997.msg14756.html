"And if I may over extend my question without severe reprimend,  I would like to know how you folks save data such as email, contact lists, documents and such when changing distrobutions.   I would like to try Sarge again (now that I know a little bit more) but don't want to lose my recent emails and documents as I did when I switched from SuSe to Debian."

Well I can answer that one for you.  The simple answer is to have your /home folder on another partition.  Check out mine.
/dev/hda2      xfs    4.5G  2.5G  2.0G  57% /
/dev/hda3      xfs     87G   70G   17G  81% /home
/dev/hda1     vfat     25G   13G   12G  52% /windows

My / partition is just 5 gigs, which is plenty unless you play UT or something.  My /home partition takes up the majority of my hard drive, and for good reason!  All of my music, my videos, my documents and source code, everything that is not OS related goes here.  Note, many times, it helps to delete the .directories before installing a new OS, but you can make exceptions for settings you really want to keep, such as .xchat or .gaim, etc.

Note, see my windows partition?  If I'm gona install windows, I usually do it first, as lilo installs better over the windows boot record, than vice versa.  This leads to my windows partition usually being hda1 since it's installed first.  However, looking back on it, I wish it had been hda3, since that would have let me delete that parition, and expand my home partition if i needed the space.

I just got a new 300g partition today.  I plan to lay out my hard drive something like this:
/dev/hda1 / 5G
/dev/hda2 /home 270G (hopefully encrypted)
with 20 gigs left of used space, just incase I get the urge to install win2k and play a game or two over christmass break.

Edit:  I recommend you have a swap partition unless you have 512mb of ram or more.  With less than 256mb, I'd go for 2:1 swap to ram ratio.  With more, I'd go 1:1.  Also, there is a varible called swappiness that you can set to tell your computer when you want to swap, from 0 (no swap) to 100 (swap ALOT).  On systems that absolutely need swap, I set this to 10.  Basically, you'll want to swap only when you absolutely need to swap, unless were talking about a server...
