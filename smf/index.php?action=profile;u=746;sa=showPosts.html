this is how i have survived in the tech game since getting my ba in 1993 from uwf.

1. go where the jobs are plenty, so that if you don't have alot of experience, but hr is looking for bodies for entry level, you have a better shot. took me 2 weeks of "pounding the pavement" in atlanta. my manager who hired me is still a good friend today, 14 years later. he is a director of it down in o-town now. i'm currently a network administrator working for a top five defense contractor on eglin. who knew??? when i started looking for a job back in 1993 in atlanta, I had NO local contacts, save my uncle and friends letting me crash on the couch as I hunted for employment.  I haven't had to do that since the internet took over, and hr finds me now.

2. once you are in the door. learn everything you can. i mean volunteer for the server room cleanup. sit in those dead head vendor meetings that last all day (hopefully catered). take those accounts, or customers, or jobs that no one wants to do, and SHINE in them.

3. i'm all for small biz, but you need to get some "named" companies, w. "big" projects on your resume.. i'm not talking "helped install outlook and repaired customers .pst's and saved their shitty small business from failing". i'm talking, lead project engineer on deploying enterprise wide computer telephony integration solution and lowering tco for telecommunications infrastructre utilizing VoIP.. or even "saved company x amount of dollars" by "integrating network faxing solutions which reduced time/energy/effort/logistics/equipment/paper/maintenance costs to the company" or "onsite support dba/lan/wan administrator for a Windows 2000/ASP/IIS based intranet registration solution supporting up to 200,000 processed customers within three-five days" (that's one of mine btw).

4. as for the certs. i've run into plenty of "paper tigers" in my days. been burned by a couple of them, but was able to recover (thank God we backed up the ms sql database every night, but thats another story).

5. college degree gets your foot in the door. what you do w. the job once you are in (the things BEYOND doing above and beyond in the job, such as making industry contacts, expanding your infinite network via yahoo! gmail, myspace, LinkedIn (great resource) participating in local trade or industry groups or WHATEVER) you need to CONTINUE to network w. others in your field, and find out where the jobs will be BEFORE they becone available, so that when it comes time to hop the lily pad (which that time always comes, whether its 4 years or 14 years) you'll be ready to leap.

That's about it! The technical stuff, if you are intelligent, thoughtful, aggressive, and a "go to" guy, you'll do well. Big sacrifices up front, going the distance and more will reap BIG rewards. Trust me, I'm living it.

-A
