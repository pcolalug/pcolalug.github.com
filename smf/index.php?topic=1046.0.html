Just because it has a shiny cool-looking interface doesn't mean it's worth the cost of the CD it's burned on.  Conversely, just because it doesn't have a cool-looking interface doesn't mean it's worthless.  Judging by that criteria, Windows XP is the greatest thing since sliced bread, and FreeBSD is worthless, when exactly the opposite is true.  Think about it - would you like developer's time spent in making things look good, or be stable and functional?

Maybe I've set up too many servers running Linux and FreeBSD to bother much with how the interface looks - I'm an old curses guy, anyway

If you're speaking of the desktop, that's a different issue, but I don't see Linux or any other non-Windows platform on the typical users desktop for a long time to come, regardless of flavor - developers seem to spend a whole lot of time making things look pretty and not a lot of time making things functional, and none of the developers working on any of the distros seem to understand the first thing about human interface design issues.  For example, look at how things are arranged - no logical presentation order or grouping, the admin apps are inconsistent and they don't expose all the functionality, and I could go on and on.

The fact of the matter is, it's silly to be arguing about which distro is the best when, from a user perspective, they *all* suck - and that's why the overwhelming majority of end users run Windows, and will for quite some time to come.  In other words, until developers stop drooling over the latest-and-greatest shiny new doodad and start paying attention to what users want, especially in terms of what's useful and needed on the enterprise desktop, Linux on the desktop will go nowhere.  Sure, business applications aren't sexy - but they are the key to getting Linux into the workplace and onto people's desktops.
