Title: Re: Counter-Attack!
Post by: rugmonster on February 09, 2007, 09:10:37 pm
This is such a bad idea on so many different levels.

First, by taking out the drones, you are likely taking out the computers of folks that don't realize what their systems are doing.

Second, there are way too many drones out there to make a dent in them.

Third, legally, you would be playing on the same grounds as the folks running the botnets, thus being in the United States, be subject to United States laws and United States fines and jails.

Fourth, so you start taking out nodes and draw the attention of the person controlling the botnet and all of the sudden, you are the target of a DDoS. Your ISP gets pissed at you and drops your service and holds you liable for damages. Yes, it's a stretch for the damages, not so much the dropping of service.

Fifth, you're spreading of fear is uncalled for. Traffic systems, hospitals, critical infrastructures that are on IP based networks are typically off Internet connected networks. If you know of any that are accessible via the Internet, even through a firewall, then you need to let someone know the error of their ways, both as a responsible citizen and a network security professional.

In case you hadn't heard, the USAF recently changed their mission to Air, Space, and Cyberspace Superiority. Leave it to the professionals. If you are so bothered by it, do a whois on the IP and send an email to or call the technical contact for the network. Notify your ISP and let them handle it. If you are actually compromised, contact the authorities, though likely won't do you any good if the attacker is from outside the US. Vigilantism isn't any more right on the Internet than it is in the real world. If you don't need to provide services to users outside the US, then block those IP blocks. It's not hard to find out what they are.

Just like you lock up your car and your house to keep people out of your stuff, why should you expect anything different for your computers? The Internet falls in no one jurisdiction. By its nature, you cannot realistically control it. If there were a way, I doubt the RIAA or MPAA would be spending money on adverts to convince people that pirating music and movies is bad.

Lastly, the root servers were not hacked. Three were targeted (http://hosted.ap.org/dynamic/stories/I/INTERNET_ATTACKS?SITE=WIRE&SECTION=HOME&TEMPLATE=DEFAULT) by botnets (http://www.us-cert.gov/current/current_activity.html#dnsanom) and were DDoS'd (http://dnsmon.ripe.net/dns-servmon/domain/plot?domain=root&day=5&month=2&year=2007&hour=16&period=48h&plot%2F=SHOW---). If you have something that states otherwise, I would like to see it.

If you look at page 2 of the SecurityFocus article, you find the following:

Quote
No digital contagion has been fingered in the latest incident, said Terry Johnson, spokesman for the Tennessee Valley Authority, the public power company that runs the Browns Ferry power plant.

"The integrated control system (ICS) network is not connected to the network outside the plant, but it is connected to a very large number of controllers and devices in the plant," Johnson said. "You can end up with a lot of information, and it appears to be more than it could handle."

The device responsible for flooding the network with data appears to be a programmable logic controller (PLC) connected to the plant's Ethernet network, according to an NRC information notice on the incident (PDF). The PLC controlled Unit 3's condensate demineralizer -- essentially a water softener for nuclear plants. The flood of data spewed out by the malfunctioning controller caused the variable frequency drive (VFD) controllers for the recirculation pumps to hang.

Such failures are common among PLC and supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems, because the manufacturers do not test the devices' handling of bad data, said Dale Peterson, CEO of industrial system security firm DigitalBond.

"What is happening in this marketplace is that vendors will build their own (network) stacks to make it cheaper," Peterson said. "And it works, but when (the device) gets anything that it didn't expect, it will gag."

In many cases, a simple vulnerability scan will even cause the devices to crash, Peterson said. During tests in an electrical substation, Nessus running in safe scan mode crashed devices, he said. In some cases, sending out broadcast data on the network will crash several of connected devices, he added.

"If you were to test any control systems that have any more than three or four different network-connected devices, they could be knocked over very easily," Peterson said.

To me, it sounds like that has nothing to do with any external malicious attack and more to do with crappy equipment.
