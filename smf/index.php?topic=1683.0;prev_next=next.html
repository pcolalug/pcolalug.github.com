Here are three reviews of Xandros 2.0:

http://www.extremetech.com/article2/0,3973,1447712,00.asp

http://www.madpenguin.org/Article811.html?POSTNUKESID=8cfb81782850b35c7b1266009b893fba

http://www.osnews.com/story.php?news_id=5665


My initial thoughts are thus, Linux is still tough for Grandma on the desktop.  Ok, gee, most folks knew this already, so let's skip to my impressions from a techie standpoint.

If I didn't want to highly customize every aspect of my desktop. . .  If I just wanted everything to work with minimal work by myself, then this is the OS for you.  I remember repeating "neat, it just works" over and over.

I had one major problem with Xandros and I really can't fault Xandros and one minor problem with its inherent philosophy which I can't fault Xandros  

I have the Best Buy Matix VPR 226R desktop system with a nVidia GeForce 4 Ti4400 and Hauppauge WINTV+FM TV tuner.  (Quick rundown of the specs includes: P4 2.26GHz, 512MB ram, SB Live Value!, IEEE1394, USB 2.0, 2 onboard ATA controllers, one with RAID.)

The first major problem was with installation.  I took a Leap of Faith (TM) and told the nice install wizard software to install on my dual booting system without touching Windows.  Interestingly, it wouldn't touch any drive with an NTFS partition on it, including the drive where I wanted Xandros to install root.  So, it installed on a drive connected to the RAID card which got me into a chicken or egg situation.  Xandros couldn't boot afterwards because it couldn't get to the drive connected to the RAID card because it couldn't load the driver for the card.  Bummer, no express install for me.

So, I went with the customized install and that was equally a breeze.  I thought the disk partition section was reasonably intuitive.

Once installed and booting, I was impressed with the uniformity of the desktop and that there were not 150 applications showing up in my menus.  Sometimes simplicity is just best and initially you shouldn't scare the heck out of the end user.

It is important to check out fan forums and fan sites to see other software like Gnome and such if you need applications that only the other desktop alternative can provide.  (GQ was one for me.)  A list of those sites are in Xandros' support area.

I did a couple of quick tests with the TV Time application and XMMS to confirm that multimedia worked out of the box.  I will try to remember to test a DVD later, but I suspect that it works.

I didn't test the Codeweaver application as I don't have a version of Office handy to test it with.  Sorry about that, but I have heard good things about it.

Enemy Territory and UT2004 demo worked right out of the install binary.  Really!  Which leads me to a point I forgot to mention.  I didn't have to update the nVidia driver because the one from nVidia is installed by default and worked great.

The other minor issues I had may be due to my lack of experience with the way Debian manages packages or fear that upgrading packages might take me into dependency heck.  I would have to have upgraded KDE, but of course, you can't, or it isn't a non-trivial process and laced with issues of dependencies that I just inherently knew would break my install.  Kopote, the multi-protocol IM client didn't work with Yahoo either, which means they are lagging on an essential component.

One of the neat features is the Xandros Networks feature which allows you to grab software from a central repository for installation similar to the Windows Update feature, but beefier.  Not only will it update your system right down to the kernel (which it did painlessly) but you can modify the apt-get source file to have the application grab software from other apt repositories.

Getting back to my other minor problem...I felt confined and I am not sure I can quantify that especially coming from a OS X user.  Ahem.

So those are just some of my thoughts on Xandros after a few days.  My work horse is still my Powerbook and the desktop is still primarily XP.  If I find myself in Xandros more over the next month, I might keep it; if not, I might offer it up to someone that wants to use Linux as a business desktop that just works.

Thanks for reading.
