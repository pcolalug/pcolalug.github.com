Quote from: imnes
Someone has to step in and stop this or otherwise, even if the suit turns up completely false and unfounded in 4 years or whenever it's resolved, the damage will have already been done.

And this is apparently what Redhat is doing.

I don't doubt that there will be a smoking crater left where SCO's nasdaq listing was after IBM gets through with them.  But the SCO vs. IBM trial isn't scheduled until sometime in 2005. So that means 2 years that SCO can continue to drag Linux through the mud.

The general gist of the suit filed by Redhat is that they are telling SCO: "Either show us what you've got or shut the hell up."  So this could be a key turning point in the whole Linux-SCO fiasco, since SCO will be required to point out just what it is that infringes.  One of two things can arise from this:

1) There is no infringing code and the folks at SCO have just been talking out of their collective ass the whole time.  End Result: The problem goes away completely, since SCO has nothing to further pursue in court (either with IBM or Linux users).

2) There is some infringing code. Just what pieces infringe is something that SCO will have to make public, with no NDA or other legal strings attached.  The offending code gets removed by the Linux community, and SCO can no longer pursue licenses from end-users, suing distro makers (such as Redhat, Mandrake, et. al) for damages, etc.  It then becomes a matter strictly between IBM and SCO.

In fact, SCO most likely won't be able to pursue damages from anyone. At all.  They themselves still distribute the allegedly infringing Linux kernel sources under the GPL, and they have made no attempts to rectify the situation.  This makes any damages worth exactly $0.00.

Case in point,  take a look at IBM-vs-SCO.  This matter (and any other case involving SCO) will be pursued in a civil court, not a criminal court.   And in a civil case, if there are damages, the plaintiff  (SCO in this case) has to show that they have made due and diligent effort to either negate or minimize said damages as much as they can. This would entail that SCO would have to identify the infringing sections, so that they could be removed by Linux developers as soon as possible.  They have not done this; ergo, they cannot collect damages.

Well, here's an update on the whole SCO situation, for those of you that haven't grown sick of this whole ordeal yet.


In this article, SCO alleges that the GPL is invalid -- a claim that is complete and utter bull, according to the rest of the world.  SCO is relying upon a rather naive interpretation of copyright law, which will never stand up in court, and I'm fairly certain that SCO knows this.  



Both here and here are pictures of slides that were reportedly leaked from a recent SCO expo.

The code shown does indeed come from Linux..  It's part of this file from the IA-64 branch.   The first slide also shows a side-by-side comparison with some obscured System V code, to show that comments were lifted 'line-by-line' and put into Linux.

This would seem like pretty damning evidence, and quite honestly, I would agree with such a sentiment.  The exact same comments can be found in several other Unices, such as BSD 2.11 from 1986.  The routine itself is even older than this, coming from the original code written by none other Ken Thompson himself, circa 1973.  In this 30-year period, said code has been reproduced in a number of academic texts, and is now in the public domain for all practical purposes.

The second  slide lacks a coresponding System V section for comparsion, but the problem with SCO using this as evidence is two-fold:  First, this code is part of something that System V could *NEVER* do to begin with, and second: this code in question was contributed to the kernel by SGI, not IBM.

So what's left: a piece of 30-year old public-domain code, and something else that wasn't even made or owned by SCO.


Sorry, SCO, but I call bollocks on this one.

Apple in court dispute over Unix

Quote


As legal battles heat up over who owns the rights to the Unix operating system, another dispute has been quietly simmering over the rights to the Unix name itself.
Apple Computer is being sued by The Open Group, the San Francisco company that claims ownership of the Unix trademark, for using the term Unix in conjunction with its Mac OS X operating system without a license. Apple has countersued, asking a judge to declare that the trademark is invalid, because the term Unix has become generic.

Though initiated nearly 18 months ago, the case has not yet gone to trial. According to a motion filed with the court Tuesday, both companies want to have an exchange of factual documents completed by August, with a trial sought for February.

 

This legal battle, though separate from SCO's recent claim that Linux uses copyrighted Unix source code, adds further fire to the debate over the custody of Unix--the 30 plus-year old OS originally developed by AT&T.

Despite SCO's acquisition, from Novell, of much of the intellectual property associated with Unix, the Unix name itself was transferred to The Open Group, which established a licensing program for the use of the name that includes software testing as well as a licensing fee, among other things. SCO and Novell are sparring over the extent of SCO's ownership of Unix, while SCO and IBM are embroiled in a trade-secrets lawsuit.

The Open Group, also known as X/Open, sued Apple in December 2001 alleging, among other things, that Apple had infringed on its trademark. The case, filed in U.S. District Court in San Jose, Calif., has been wending its way through the courts, with the parties still in the discovery process. Months of mediation meetings ended without a settlement, according to The Open Group.

Since introducing Mac OS X in March 2001, Apple has consistently touted the Unix underpinnings as part of its marketing of the operating system. Apple's Web site, for example, has a page devoted to the Unix base of the OS, including a logolike GIF that shows a metal plate bearing the words "Unix Based."

"That's kind of the one that we are least fond of," said Open Group Chief Operating Officer Steve Nunn, who notes that the group has had a similar Unix license logo of its own for some time.

The Open Group wants Apple to have Mac OS X undergo testing to certify that it complies with its standards for software bearing the Unix name; it also wants Apple to pay a fee. The Open Group says the costs to license the name are reasonable, based on the size of the company and the rough number of copies of the software Apple sells. In any case, no company is required to pay more than $110,000, said Graham Bird, vice president of marketing for The Open Group.

"Apple would be at the top of that range because they ship a lot of units," Bird said. "It's not like we are talking about bankrupting the company."

Apple argues it should be free to use the term as it sees fit, noting that there are many flavors of Unix, including FreeBSD, and that one should not have to submit to The Open Group's testing to use the term.

"Apple does not use the term Unix as a trademark in connection with Apple's product," the company said in court papers seen by CNET News.com. "Apple accurately uses the generic term Unix merely to identify or describe an aspect or feature of Apple's Mac OS X operating system. This is consistent with past and current industry standards."
