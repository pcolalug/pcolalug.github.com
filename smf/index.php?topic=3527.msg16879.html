Well......  There were 6 people at the meeting, including me.  We chewed the fat for a while and then discussed the backup solutions being used by those present.  I handed out printed copies of my backup script and discussed it briefly.  There was a short discussion also about monitoring/keylogging software.

This was the first meeting I'd been to in a while.  I was hoping for something more.  We talked about the past and future of the LUG, and how much it's changed since the "glory days".  I am still struggling to grasp why there isn't more interest and attendance, but cannot come up with the answers.  We speculated that perhaps it's due to Linux being easier to use today than it was 3-4 years ago, and that folks don't need so much help from other users as we used to.  I believe that is a big part of it, along with the vast amount of help/info that is now available on the internet.

I wish we could generate some renewed interest and participation.  We talked about possibly holding some sequential "training" classes about the basics/intro level stuff, to help new Linux users or potential converts make the transition from Windows.  I think that would be a great idea, and would certainly be willing to help with presentations and events.  I have quite a few presentations already done from years past, which could be dusted off and presented again.  The sticking point to that idea is to actually get anyone to show up.  We've tried flyers posted around town before, with less than stellar results.  Most other "advertising" would cost money.  I honestly don't know how else we can "get the word out" and increase awareness and interest in using Linux/OSS.

If anyone has ideas on how to do this, please share.  Again, I would like to get re-involved with the LUG, but am not real interested in attending meetings where the same 4 people show up each time, and there's nothing beneficial to anyone being accomplished.  If anyone else is willing to do anything to try and advance the LUG, please speak up.  Now.

Thanks,

I agree with danc3 that we may need to re-think what our purpose and mission of the LUG is. I totally agree that there may not be as much need to lean on one another on a regular basis to get a Linux system up and running like there used to be. And I also see that as a good thing.

With the great on-line resources available today and the ease at which one can learn to work with Linux, it makes the day-to-day hand holding less necessary. Linux is just getting easier. Yay!

However, that makes us have to consider what our purpose is. What do we want to accomplish as a LUG? I have enjoyed the different topics and presentations at meetings. But with a specific topic, it may cause some people to be either intimidated by the subject or not see the need to attend. I think that will always cause members to evaluate whether a specific meeting is needful to them. Some of us attend every meeting possible regardless of the topic. But, not everyone can attend every meeting. As most of you know, I am one of those people. Fortunately, the schedule works out that I will be home for at least the next 2 and possibly the next 4 meetings. I am interested in banging out a direction with all of you before we have elections again. We can bring in the next administration with a new set of goals.

There will always be new people that we can mentor and help along, like OlRedHair. We never want to neglect them. We were all there once.

It's true that we need to figure out some way to breathe new life in to the LUG.  I honestly find the meetings to often be very dry, I attend both out of habit, and for the conversation more than anything.  I miss the LAN parties, there just wasn't enough attendance to justify keeping that going.  It's more than needing something fun once in a while, too.

Every meeting needs to engage the mind and keep the interest of both the old hat crowd, the people who can do the advanced stuff, and the new generation of people who are having no problem getting Linux installed with distributions that are designed with user friendliness first in mind.  Perhaps we don't need only a 'newbie' course.  We still need more advanced topics covered to keep interest with the more experienced members.

From what I've seen, there also isn't a whole lot of interaction between the steering committee, and the general membership.  Each meeting is just randomly tossed together these days, usually on a whim.  We should strive to bring back that communication as well.  We lost something big when that faded away.  It's not that the steering committee isn't listening, either.  If you make a post, we read it.  There just aren't enough dialogues started, by anyone.  We need to revive that, and the committee should lead the way.

I don't have the answers, but I have ideas.  Most of them revolve around getting people talking again, and trying to get past some of the animosity that was generated the past two years.  Let's just be civil, because the opposite never helps.
